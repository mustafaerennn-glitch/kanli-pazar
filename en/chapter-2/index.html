Chapter 2

Toward Kanlı Pazar Leftist Youth Movements in Turkey Between 1960 and 1970

pp. 103–187
📎 Mustafa Kemal March for a Fully Independent Turkey — 1968
Added: 18 April 2026

Looking at the trajectory of leftist youth from the 1960s into the 1970s, one sees that, after passing through the stations of the CHP, TİP, and the National Democratic Revolution (MDD), they went on to found their own armed revolutionary movements. Youth who had set out to protect the Principles of Atatürk would, from the second half of the 1960s onward, come under TİP's influence and take up socialist ideals. These socialist ideals would, in turn, transform into armed revolutionary movements under the influence, on one hand, of the wave of revolution that broke out in Europe in the spring of 1968, and, on the other, of the revolutionary, socialist movements of Asia, Africa, and Latin America — Palestine, Vietnam, and Cuba above all.181

Characteristics of the Youth Movement Between 1960 and 1965

Harun Karadeniz states, in his book Olaylı Yıllar ve Gençlik [Turbulent Years and Youth], that the youth movements of 1960–1965 were almost uniform in character. He describes this "character" as "assimilating the Principles of Atatürk to 27 May, and taking ownership of and keeping alive whatever had until then been called revolution." "In other words, the youth movements between 1960 and 1965 were progressive in nature, taking a stand against conduct hostile to the Principles of Atatürk and to 27 May."183

It was only after 1965 that words such as "exploitation," "fascism," and "imperialism" began appearing frequently in the declarations of youth organizations. It was also in these years that the fikir kulüpleri [thought clubs]184 founded at universities in the 1950s became more active, and new clubs began to be founded under various names. Karadeniz says these clubs were founded "generally by intellectuals who had adopted leftist thought."185 The most active of these clubs was the Siyasal Bilgiler Fakültesi Fikir Kulübü [Faculty of Political Science Thought Club], founded at Ankara University on 4 December 1954. This club would also lead the founding of the Fikir Kulüpleri Federasyonu (FKF), which was established on 16 December 1965 through "the coming-together of 5 thought clubs affiliated with Ankara University, dominated by TİP-leaning youth."186

TİP's influence is evident in the FKF's founding, following its success in the 10 October 1965 general election, in which it won 15 parliamentary seats. After the election, TİP encouraged the spread of thought clubs in order to increase its influence among university youth. The Ankara-based FKF also carried out organizing work in Istanbul; this work resulted in the formation of a 13-member provisional committee in April 1966,187 and the founding of the FKF Istanbul Secretariat on 21 May 1966.188 The secretary of the Istanbul Secretariat was Veysi Sarısözen, Secretary of TİP's Istanbul Youth Branch.189

Transformation After 1965

1965 brought important changes for the youth movement. It was the year in which the socialist worldview, which had been spreading among intellectuals since 1960, began to be reflected in the youth movements. In saying this, I am not speaking of individual young people becoming socialists one by one, but trying to characterize the structure of youth organizations in general. Even before 1965, the socialist worldview had already begun to find adherents, and even to be adopted, among young people. But it was specifically within 1965 that the socialist worldview's reflection in the youth movements took place.190

This transformation after 1965 can be seen in the variety and content of the pieces appearing in the 17th issue, dated 1 February 1965, of Devrim Gençliği [Revolutionary Youth], the publication of the Türkiye Milli Talebe Federasyonu (TMTF): "Land Reform, Turkish-Soviet Relations, Turkey's Oil Problem, the TİP Meeting, Iran, Demirel and Beyond, Reaction, the African Revolution, Vietnam, Italy's Presidential Elections, the Private Sector and Foreign Capital, the Ereğli (Iron and Steel) Plunder, Economic Unification and the Labor Movement…"191

The famous "Johnson Letter," which holds a place in our political history, also played a part in the transformation that took place in 1965. The letter, sent by U.S. President Johnson to Prime Minister İnönü on 5 June 1964 with the aim of preventing Turkey's military intervention in Cyprus, warned — in a "crude," "humiliating" tone, almost as if scolding Turkey — that such an operation should not be undertaken, that if it were, the weapons the U.S. had given Turkey could not be used for it, and that NATO would not protect Turkey against any possible Soviet intervention prompted by it. In the same year as this humiliating letter, İnönü gave his well-known statement to Time magazine: "One day, a new world will be built under new conditions, and Turkey will find its own place in that world too."192 This process found an echo among the youth as well. A declaration published under the signatures of the chairmen of the TMTF, the İstanbul Üniversitesi Talebe Birliği, and the İstanbul Yüksek Okullar Talebe Birliği demanded "the withdrawal of the [U.S.] ambassador, so that Turkish-American relations would not take a dangerous course."193

The Magazine Dönüşüm and the Attacks Carried Out on Demirel's Orders

One of the signs of youth's turn toward socialism and their coming under TİP's influence was the magazine Dönüşüm [Transformation], first published on 22 April 1965. İsmet Özel, one of the SBF FK's administrators at the time, describes this magazine — published by "TİP-affiliated youth, most of them members of the SBF Fikir Kulübü" — as follows:194

We had a stance of wanting, for the first time, to run something ourselves — to publish a magazine ourselves, sell it ourselves — and we planned an action along these lines and published Dönüşüm Dergisi. It was a core priority of ours that the initiative belong to socialists. (…) We earned more in sales revenue than we had expected. In fact, we even founded the FKF with the money we made from Dönüşüm.

İsmet Özel

The magazine's first issue, whose publisher of record was Ataol Behramoğlu of the Faculty of Language, History and Geography (DTCF), carried pieces by Sadun Aren, Aziz Nesin, and Cem Eroğul, along with translated works. The magazine's editorial stated that it "would always be the voice, at home and abroad, of the people, of the exploited, of the oppressed, would attend to the reality of the university, and would explain why those who claimed to represent the youth were in fact mere tools."195

Published biweekly, the magazine drew attention when it went on sale in Kızılay Square in Ankara and Taksim Square in Istanbul under slogans such as "Dönüşüm, Against Exploitation, Imperialism, and Fascism," "Dönüşüm, the Voice of Socialist Youth, of the University Student," "Writing About America's Barbarism in Vietnam," "Sadun Aren, Aziz Nesin, Mehmet Ali Aybar Write in Dönüşüm," "Dönüşüm, Against the Rotary Press, Against the Press Barons," "The Newspaper of the Intellectual and the Worker," "Dönüşüm, the Magazine of Socialist and Intellectual Youth" — and it unsettled the right-wing AP-CKMP-MP-YTP coalition then in power. The young people selling the magazine came under attack. There are accounts and claims that Demirel himself personally ordered these attacks. Researcher and writer Suat Parlar relates one of these attacks through a long excerpt he takes from Ahmet Kahraman's book Devri Süleyman [The Süleyman Era], characterizing it as "the signal that a civil war had begun":196

Baltacıoğlu had told it this way (journalist İsmail Baltacıoğlu): in the spring of 1965, TİP was publishing a magazine called Dönüşüm. University youth sold the magazine in Kızılay Square in the evenings, at the hour when civil servants were heading home. As the young people sold the magazine on the street, they also chanted slogans: against exploitation, imperialism, fascism, transformation.

It was said, in AP circles, that Süleyman Demirel — who called these young people communists — was disturbed by what they were doing. One day, at AP headquarters, the phone rang. Barlas Küntay, Chairman of the Youth Branches, answered. In an excited voice, he said, "At your command, Mr. Chairman," and hung up. There were a few of us there. A few people from the youth branches, and myself. Since I worked at a pro-AP newspaper, they counted me as one of their own. Küntay said: that was Süleyman Bey. He says the communists have gone too far, that they're disturbing the public in Kızılay Square in the evenings. He's ordering that the necessary be done… He immediately called the youth branch administrators to a meeting… Barlas Küntay announced the chairman's instructions: Our chairman's instructions are definite. We will not surrender the square to the communists. We will disperse them. The police, too, have been given the necessary instructions to assist us. Now I want you to gather as many men as you can find.

The AP youth branches' preparations for the attack continued for days. Idle, unemployed, hulking men were hired from the shantytowns of Altındağ. The assault force thus formed was meant to be reinforced and strengthened with university youth… Help was requested from the CKMP, the coalition partner then under Türkeş's leadership. An alliance was struck with the CKMP to fight the communists.

Ahmet Kahraman, quoted in: Suat Parlar

The attack took place on 8 June 1965. A group chanting slogans such as "Nationalist Turkey" and "Down with the Communists" attacked the young people selling the magazine with the clubs in their hands. The police joined in this attack as well. The attack was led by Yılmaz Yalçıner, Chairman of the CKMP Youth Branches.197 Following the attack, the Dönüşüm-affiliated youth who had been attacked were taken into custody. Among those detained were Uğur Mumcu, Ataol Behramoğlu, Hüseyin Ergun, and Orhan Apaydın.

Newspapers published on 13 June reported that the government's "Committee for the Struggle Against Extreme Currents" had convened and discussed "leftist currents" and the "Kızılay Events."198 Following this work, a new bill was drafted to combat currents characterized as "extreme." What happened fit the saying that the thief accuses the homeowner first. The SBF FK published a declaration on this bill:199

The Minister of the Interior demonstrated a fresh instance of an anti-democratic method by submitting to the Council of Ministers a report demanding "immediate and decisive measures" against extreme leftist currents. The fabricated term "extreme leftism," used in reality to suppress the progressive views in our country, is in fact a symptom of the anxiety that arises from university youth attending to the problems of the homeland. (…) What all these preparations are ultimately aimed at achieving is plain: that the youth sit quietly, not cry out against those who steal and bargain over the country's wealth, not defend freedom and democracy. (…) We declare to the Turkish people, in the birth pangs of happier days, that we stand against exploitation and imperialism always, and will remain upright even at the cost of our own existence.

Declaration of the SBF Fikir Kulübü

After 5 issues, Dönüşüm suspended publication, on the grounds that the debates and clashes stirred up around the magazine served the interests of "the juntaists."200 The magazine's 5th issue addressed the attacks against it directly:201

The incidents provoked during our magazine's biweekly sale in Kızılay have shown an increasing tendency to develop and clarify. (…) DÖNÜŞÜM is a symbol of the years in which Turkey came to know an unbanned left. In a sense, it is the first step of the youth movements on the left.

Dönüşüm magazine, Issue 5

After suspending publication, Dönüşüm was relaunched under the editorship of Abdullah Nefes, but its second life did not last long either.

From Progressive–Reactionary Polarization to Rightist–Leftist Polarization

As the youth movement took on a "socialist" character after 1965, the nature of the divide within the youth began to change as well. "The division that, up to 1965, had mostly taken the form of progressive versus reactionary youth, slowly took, after 1965, the form of leftist versus reactionary, and increasingly, rightist versus leftist."202

As the youth described as "leftist" drifted away from the CHP and grew closer to TİP, the youth described as "rightist" fell under the influence of the AP and the CKMP. The AP made use of the CKMP's active members as well, putting them forward for its own purposes while turning a blind eye to their unlawful conduct.203

Necip Fazıl Kısakürek compares this turn of youth toward TİP, and thus toward socialism, to "scabies" or "the Muscovite cold," and says that there are now only two generations left:204

The most terrifying disease this revolution has produced is the scabies of socialism… As I mentioned a moment ago, I call it scabies, or the Muscovite cold… It has swept over everything, the way the Spanish flu once did, engulfing the whole world. (…) Yes! There are now only two generations left: one, the communists, and the other, us, the rootists…

Necip Fazıl Kısakürek

Likening "communism" to a disease was, in that period, a widespread stance in nationalist-conservative, traditionalist circles. Sezai Karakoç, too, characterizes communism as a "mania of hunger" and "humanity's neurosis":205

Communism is a mania of hunger. The worldview, in that moment, of a person whom hunger has struck to the head. Communism is a NETWORK OF CONVENIENCES the maniac has invented to satisfy himself and to silence his own crises. (…) Communism is a neurosis of humanity. And the eruptions of this neurosis are world wars.

Sezai Karakoç

The meanings Karakoç attaches to the terms "right" and "left" are also worth noting. In his writings from 1963–1964, Karakoç grounds these terms in the Quran, adding:206

In Islamic thought, the rightist is the believer — that is, the mü'min — the one who carries Islamic belief, thought, and appearance. In other words, the constructive person. The leftist, by contrast, is the denier, the rejecter — one whose beliefs, thoughts, and appearance stand not only against Islam but against anyone who is not Islamic. In other words, the destructive person.

Sezai Karakoç

In other writings, Karakoç continues attaching negative meanings to the left, likening the right to "art" and the left to mere craft, or saying that the left represents "Satan." According to Karakoç, Adam's being in Paradise represented a rightist mode of existence, but Satan, representing the left, entered the picture, and the fall from Paradise took place. The Fall is the revolution of the left.207

1966: Turning Organizations "Two-Headed," and Clashes

The FKF's organizing efforts intensified in 1966. Five more thought clubs joined the FKF.208 The FKF held film days, and seminars were organized with the participation of TİP's leading figures.

The standout action of 1966 was the "Dean Rusk Affair." U.S. Secretary of State Rusk came to Ankara on 19 April to attend CENTO meetings. The FKF decided to protest "American imperialism, NATO, and CENTO." During the protest demonstrations, 74 young people were taken into custody and referred to court. The detained students were released on the bail of Prof. Dr. Muammer Aksoy.209 Rusk's visit also led to clashes between FKF- and TİP-affiliated youth and rightist youth. A day after the visit, a clash broke out between FKF youth protesting the detentions and Rusk, and youth affiliated with the Milliyetçi Türk Gençlik Teşkilatı, led by Yılmaz Yalçıner. Two FKF students were stabbed, and there were injuries on both sides. Young people staging a protest in Kızılay in front of AP headquarters and the USIS (American Information Center) were attacked by people shouting, "Do you want to sell the country to the communists?" Another clash took place in front of the U.S. Embassy Library.210 While all these clashes were taking place, the MTTB's deputy chairman issued a statement saying, "The hospitable Turkish youth condemns this accursed incident, and welcomes Dean Rusk."211

Another protest directed at America took place at the end of the year. On 12 November, the FKF took part in a rally called "The Turkish People's Uprising Against the Americans," organized by the Türkiye İş Sendikası to protest the pressure placed on workers employed at American bases. At the rally's end, protesters continuing to demonstrate in front of the USIS and trying to breach the barricade were attacked by "AP members and nationalist youth" chanting "down with the communists." 15 people were wounded, and 26 of those who took part in the rally were detained. Demirel declared that "those who sing songs of independence in the streets are engaged in treachery and delusion," while 6 of those detained were placed under arrest.212

Participation in this rally took place against the backdrop of the tug-of-war within the FKF between TİP-influenced youth advocating "Socialist Revolution" (SD) and youth advocating "National Democratic Revolution" (MDD). A faction within the FKF did not support the FKF's participation in the rally.213

Faced with youth increasingly becoming a dynamic force and turning toward socialism under TİP's influence, the AP government tried to bring these organizations under its own control by making them "two-headed":214

The turning of the İnşaat Fakültesi Talebe Cemiyeti [Civil Engineering Faculty Student Association] into a two-headed body was only the beginning; as I mentioned earlier, after the İnşaat Cemiyeti, the İTÜ Talebe Birliği and, in 1966, the TMTF were made two-headed, with at least one faction, in each case, serving as the government's mouthpiece within that organization. For the AP government, the matter of turning organizations two-headed was not confined to youth organizations. As is known, the Union of Chambers was later made two-headed as well. Necmettin Erbakan, chairman of the faction opposed to the government of the day, was removed from the Union of Chambers by police intervention.

To summarize briefly, an important point worth dwelling on is the method that governments apply — and can reapply at any moment — against organizations not of their own making. When a government turns an organization two-headed, it has, at the very least, sabotaged any work within it that might work against its own interests.

Harun Karadeniz

Youth Actions in 1967

1967 was the year in which youth began openly declaring their "socialist" identity, and, as they saw it a requirement of this identity, took part in workers' and peasants' actions. This can be seen clearly in a piece titled "Socialist Youth Action," appearing in the second issue, dated 18 January 1967, of Kavga Bülteni [Struggle Bulletin],215 the FKF's publication:216

We, socialist youth, know that socialism in Turkey will be built together with the people. It is clear that conduct and struggles cut off from their roots, separate from the people, will bring no benefit to us, to our people. For years, youth and intellectuals have always positioned themselves against the people, finding their dress and their way of life strange, always finding themselves at odds with the people in every effort they made. Because they were never able to attend to the underlying realities.

Our greatest task today is to show the people, through our actions as well, that we are separate from the "snobbish youth," the "necktie-wearing intellectual" set. The people have always stood against these two strata. The surface-level reason for this opposition may seem quite trivial. But it is clear that, at bottom, the reasons lie in "the bread question, the struggle to survive"; the people know these people as those who oppose their "vote." Socialist Youth must be able to show that they are separate from these, that they always stand together with the people.

Kavga Bülteni, 18 January 1967

The word "socialism" was now frequently found in youth's declarations, and their actions reached new dimensions in 1967. Boycotts were organized. The İstanbul Teknik Üniversitesi Talebe Birliği (İTÜ TB), the İstanbul Teknik Üniversitesi Teknik Okulu Talebe Birliği (İTÜTO TB), the İstanbul Yüksek Teknik Okulu Talebe Birliği (İYTO TB), and the Orta Doğu Teknik Üniversitesi Öğrenci Birliği (ODTÜ ÖB) were particularly active. They formed alliances among themselves, taking joint stances and joint action on many issues, and together carried out the "Private Schools Must Be Nationalized" march between Istanbul and Ankara.

Harun Karadeniz says the following about 1967:217

(…) After 1960, young people who had defended, first, freedom, and then economic issues and the country's development through an anti-imperialist stance, came, from 1967 onward, to regard standing alongside the working classes as their duty — in other words, they began to defend the interests of the working people.

Harun Karadeniz

Some of the actions and activities of youth in 1967 are as follows:

— In 1967, a strike took place at the Singer factory in Maltepe, Istanbul. This strike was broken by harsh police intervention. The TMTF, İTÜ TB, İYTO TB, İTÜTO TB, and ODTÜ ÖB took an interest in this strike and prepared a pamphlet titled "The Game Behind Singer-Branded Goods, and the Strike."

— When it was learned that olive oil rejected by Italy for containing machine oil was being sold to the public in Anatolia, a trip was made to the Aegean to investigate. The Ankara Üniversitesi Talebe Birliği (AÜ TB) printed a booklet titled "The Olive Oil Fraud and Its Aftermath."

— A health survey was carried out in the Harran Plain of the Urfa region, and a pamphlet titled "The Provinces Hell Forgot" was prepared.

— A trip was made to the village of Atalan to support the peasants in a land dispute that had broken out there between landlords and peasants.

— The İTÜ TB printed the 1960 Constitution with the words "The Constitution Is Open to Socialism" on its cover.

— After a bill was submitted to parliament proposing that land that had lost its forest status be returned to its former owners, and after Americans showed interest in acquiring certain forest areas in the Black Sea Region, the FKF organized a march titled "The Forests Belong to the People, They Cannot Be Privatized," beginning at Tandoğan Square and ending at Cemal Gürsel Square, on 8 May.

— The FKF declared 19–27 May "Turkish Revolution Week" and organized several events.

— In July 1967, the TMTF assisted street vendors in organizing a march.

— In November 1967, a march for "Private Schools Must Be Nationalized" was organized from Istanbul to Ankara. The march began on 7 November and ended on 20 November. During this period, a boycott was declared at the İTÜ. Around 150 students took part in the march. Rallies were held in every province and district along the march route.

— After TİP deputy Çetin Altan's parliamentary immunity was targeted for removal, the "First Awakening Rally" was held on 8 July 1967. Despite the rally, Altan's immunity was lifted on 21 July.

1967 was also a year in which clashes and attacks increased:218

After the TMTF was made two-headed, Ekrem Özer, a rightist elected chairman at the Istanbul congress, distributed a declaration at the İTÜ TB's building in Gümüşsuyu on 2 January, and shouted, "If you interfere with the Federation's affairs, we'll make things bad for you" — whereupon the youth of the leftist-led İTÜ TB raided the MTTB, leading to a clash with MTTB members. The MTTB then raided the İTÜ TB in return. The situation continued with mutual raids. Many were wounded in the MTTB's raid on the Gümüşsuyu dormitory. Afterward, a march went first to Taksim, and two days later, with faculty members also taking part, to Beyazıt.219

Some of the protesting youth were taken into custody, and some were placed under arrest. The 10,000 lira needed to secure the arrested students' release was provided by İTÜ Rector Prof. Bedri Karafakioğlu.220221

Another important development of 1967 for the FKF and student youth was the closure and expulsion orders issued against them. Closure orders were issued against the Siyasal Bilgiler Fikir Kulübü, the Eskişehir İktisadi ve Ticari İlimler Akademisi Fikir Kulübü, and the Çapa Yüksek Öğretmen Okulu Fikir Kulübü. Administrators of the DTCF Fikir Kulübü received six-month expulsions, while founders of the Çapa Yüksek Öğretmen Okulu Fikir Kulübü (YÖO FK) — İbrahim Kaypakkaya among them — received one-month expulsions.

Another act of hostility, this one originating with university administrations, showed itself at Atatürk University in Erzurum. In the room where students belonging to the İktisat Fakültesi Fikir Kulübü stayed at the Atatürk Üniversitesi Öğrenci Yurdu, a hidden microphone was found, its cable running all the way to the dormitory administration.

The actions that defined 1967, however, were the protest actions over the Sixth Fleet's arrival and the "Eastern Rallies" organized by TİP.

June 1967: The Sixth Fleet Arrives

The Sixth Fleet anchored in Istanbul's harbor toward the end of June. Its arrival also set off protest demonstrations by leftist youth. Following the souring of relations in 1965, the Sixth Fleet's arrival was no longer welcomed, and increasingly provoked protests. First, the wreath the Sixth Fleet Command had laid at the Taksim Monument was burned, following a meeting organized by the TMTF's deputy chairman and the chairmen of the İTÜ student unions. In its place, a wreath was left bearing the words "Let Imperialism's Bloody Hand Not Reach Toward Our Ata — It Will Be Broken." The police removed this wreath an hour later. In response, students left a new wreath bearing the same inscription. On 24 June 1967, a march was organized from Beyazıt to Taksim to protest the Sixth Fleet. Passing through Dolmabahçe, the American flag flying on the quay was lowered and a Turkish flag raised in its place. More than 10,000 people took part in the rally held in Taksim. That evening, a group of young people was beaten by police in the middle of the street.

Another action carried out continually during the Sixth Fleet's time in Turkey was snatching the caps off American sailors who came ashore and wandered about. This action, in particular, continued in the evenings as a sustained campaign.

October 1967: The Sixth Fleet Returns to Istanbul

The Sixth Fleet, having left Istanbul after a stay in June, returned to Istanbul's harbors in October. Leftist youth organizations staged a sit-in at Dolmabahçe on 7 October to protest the Fleet's renewed arrival. Because of this action, American sailors could not come ashore that day.

The İTÜ TB published a declaration protesting the Sixth Fleet's arrival:222

As is known, America is sabotaging our national economy on every front, and, together with its local partners, shamelessly exploiting all our resources, both above and below ground, and is a principal cause of our underdevelopment.

Beyond this, for the spoiled American and the American soldier, Saigon and Turkish ports make little difference. They use our beautiful cities as a center for sexual release, and can pose for the cameras of the best-selling newspapers with Turkish girls in their laps.

Once again, from 7 October until the imperialist fleet leaves, an attempt will be made to make Istanbul's streets resemble the streets of Saigon.

We, Turkish youth, are resolved not to allow this this time. And as we call all nationalist forces to resist America, we believe that America's true face, as elsewhere, has now been exposed in Turkey too. This game will not go on much longer. It cannot.

The Turkish Nation will once again teach them the lesson they deserve, and, should they not depart in a civilized manner, will show the whole world once more that it is no less honorable than the people of Vietnam.

No one will be able to hold back Atatürk's nation from its struggle to reach the level of a civilized people.

If America is our enemy, we have no fear; we, too, are its enemy. The time has come to meet America with America's own methods. The future is bright, is happy. It is ours.

Declaration of the İTÜ Talebe Birliği

Youth's protest actions continued when 4 young people, members of the FKF Istanbul Secretariat, pitched a tent at Dolmabahçe on 11 October and began a hunger strike to protest the Sixth Fleet. Five hours into the hunger strike, the young people were taken into custody, and in response, a new hunger-strike tent was pitched in Taksim Park on 12 October, where five people — two of them workers — began a hunger strike. Numerous organizational leaders, along with the poets Fazıl Hüsnü Dağlarca and Şükran Kurdakul, joined this action in support.

As the Sixth Fleet's commander, Vice Admiral William I. Martin, said, "The Sixth Fleet has been coming and going from Turkey for 20 years. These visits are always made in friendship. We could not understand the meaning of this protest. We are always Turkey's friend, and wish to remain its friend,"223 the protests continued in the form of American sailors being roughed up wherever they were seen, their caps being taken, and eggs being thrown at their heads. Many protesting students were brought to court over these protests. 7 American soldiers were also referred to court, on charges of a knife attack, harassment, and fighting.224

In response to these statements and actions by the young people, writer and politician Orhan Seyfi Orhon wrote a piece in the newspaper Son Havadis defending the Sixth Fleet:225

"In my view, the only remedy is to meet communist provocations with the same weapon. Are they staging a sit-in rally to keep the friendly, allied American admiral from coming ashore at Dolmabahçe? Then let nationalist youth stage a welcoming demonstration, placards of friendship in hand! Against 'American dog, go home,' let there be 'Welcome, our American friends!' Are they attacking American students? Let us invite the American students to a soirée. Are they throwing stones? Let us hand out bouquets! Are they cursing? Let us applaud… Nationalist youth against the leftists…"

Orhan Seyfi Orhon, Son Havadis

On 15 October, a protest rally was held in İzmir as well, where ships belonging to the Sixth Fleet had anchored. The rally did not pass without incident. A hunger strike was also attempted here, but permission was not granted.

The protests and the hunger strike came to an end on 17 October, the day the last of the Sixth Fleet's ships departed, with the burning of an American flag in Taksim.

Anti-NATO Demonstrations

The final protests of 1967 targeted NATO. On 9 December 1967, an anti-"NATO and America" demonstration was organized in Ankara by the FKF, the ODTÜ ÖB, the Barış Derneği [Peace Association], the Ankara Üniversitesi Ziraat Fakültesi Öğrenci Derneği, and the SBF Öğrenci Derneği. September–October 1967: TİP's Eastern Rallies

TİP had taken an interest in the "Eastern Question" since its founding. A section of the party program adopted in 1964 was titled "Development of the East." Sadun Aren, who held a leadership role in the party and was elected a deputy from Istanbul in the 1965 election, says that although the Political Parties Law forbade parties from stating that Turkey contained minorities based on language, religion, or ethnic origin, TİP approached the matter "cautiously," while nonetheless noting that Kurdish-speaking citizens lived in the country's eastern regions, that they faced discrimination on account of this trait, and that the region was, moreover, underdeveloped.228 TİP's views on this matter would show themselves even more clearly at its 4th Congress, held on 29–30 October 1970. The decision taken at this congress can be seen as remarkably current, bold, and progressive even for 2000s Turkey:229

That the Kurdish people live in the east of Turkey, and that the ruling classes' fascist power, dominant over the Kurdish people from the outset, have applied a policy of oppression, terror, and assimilation toward them, one that has at times taken on the character of bloody persecution;

That one of the fundamental reasons the region inhabited by the Kurdish people lags behind Turkey's other regions is, in addition to capitalism's law of uneven development, a result of the economic and social policy pursued by the ruling-class governments that have taken into account the fact that the Kurdish people live in this region;

That, for this reason, treating the "Eastern question" as merely a matter of regional development is nothing but an extension of the ruling class's chauvinist-nationalist views and stance;

That it is a natural and necessary revolutionary duty for our party — the relentless enemy of every kind of antidemocratic, fascist, oppressive, chauvinist-nationalist current — to support the Kurdish people's struggle to exercise their constitutional rights of citizenship and to realize all their other democratic aspirations and demands;

That, in order to unite the Kurdish people's struggle to express and realize their developing democratic aspirations and demands with the struggle for socialist revolution carried out under the leadership of the working class and its vanguard organization, our party, into a single revolutionary wave, Kurdish and Turkish socialists must work shoulder to shoulder within the party;

That ensuring the racist-nationalist-chauvinist-bourgeois ideology applied against the Kurdish people is razed to the ground among party members, socialists, and all workers and other laboring masses is a fundamental and ongoing cause of the party's ideological struggle and development;

The party hereby accepts and declares that it approaches [the Kurdish question] from the standpoint of the requirements of the working class's struggle for socialist revolution.

Decision of TİP's 4th Congress, 29–30 October 1970

Let them learn, by asking their fellow race, the Armenians, that the Turkish race is exceedingly patient, but that when its buttermilk froths over, there is no stopping it, like Kağan Arslan — and let them come to their senses.

The Devrimci Doğu Kültür Ocakları (DDKO)

The Eastern Rallies, which began in 1967, gave rise, among Kurdish university youth, to ideas of establishing a new organization specific to "the East." İsmail Beşikçi, too, says that "national sentiments began to develop and flourish among university students after the 1967 Eastern Rallies," and that he heard of efforts made in this direction beginning in early 1968, which came to fruition in the spring of 1969.236 The Ankara DDKO and the Istanbul DDKO were founded first. These were followed by the founding of the Ergani, Silvan, Batman, and Diyarbakır DDKOs.

The founding of the DDKOs was also influenced by the Türkiye Kürdistan Demokratik Partisi (TKDP),237 founded on 11 July 1965 as the Turkish branch of Barzani's Kürdistan Demokrat Partisi (KDP), and by the autonomy agreement signed on 11 March 1970 between Iraqi President Saddam Hussein and the Kürdistan Demokrat Partisi (KDP). A third case was the Dev-Genç case.

Actions of 1968

1968 was the year in which the momentum of youth actions increased, in which actions grew steadily harsher through boycotts and occupations, and in which, with the killing of Vedat Demircioğlu, the leftist youth movement gave its first "martyr." This visible activity was only the surface of the water. It was made of waves… Underneath, there was another kind of movement, a tug-of-war. Youth began trying to move beyond the bounds of the "parliamentarism" of TİP, which was trying to keep them under its control through the FKF, and began gathering around the National Democratic Revolution (MDD) thesis and its theorist, Mihri Belli.241 This showed itself, on one hand, in the creation of organizations outside the FKF,242 and, on the other, in a power struggle within the FKF itself.

The developments of 1968 also had external causes. The excitement of the "'68 Movement," which had broken out in Europe and was beginning to sweep and shake the world in wave after wave, found an echo among the youth of our own country as well. At the same time, under the influence of the Vietnamese "war of national liberation," reactions against American imperialism produced an effect that heightened "national awakening." Following the letter U.S. President Johnson sent to Prime Minister İnönü on 5 June 1964, the anti-American sentiment that had surfaced in our country took on a new dimension in 1968. Following the anti-NATO protests first organized in Turkey in the last months of 1967, a "No to NATO Week" came onto the agenda in 1968, and the "Mustafa Kemal March" was held from Samsun to Ankara.

Another important event of 1968, one that was also a manifestation of the fragmentation within the Turkish left, was the founding of the Devrimciler Güçbirliği (Dev-Güç) [Revolutionaries' Force Union]. Following a call issued in the magazine "Türk Solu" [The Turkish Left], where Mihri Belli held influence, the "Devrimci Kuruluşlar Güçbirliği İcra Komitesi" [Executive Committee of the Force Union of Revolutionary Organizations], known as Dev-Güç, was formed on 27 March 1968 with the participation of 29 organizations. Kadri Kaplan, a Natural Senator and former MBK member, was appointed chairman of this committee. Since TİP regarded these efforts as "junta preparation," it kept its distance from Dev-Güç.243 However, because Doğu Perinçek, from the MDD camp, was elected Chairman at the FKF's 2nd Congress, held on 23–24 March 1968, the FKF Head Office decided to join Dev-Güç, and did so.244

1968 was, for every segment of the Turkish left, both a leap forward and a period of upheaval. It was in 1968 that both revolution and counter-revolution first began to manifest themselves on a mass scale.

With TİP's "exclusion" of FKF youth — running parallel to its own internal dynamic's "tendency" — this youth, drawn within the lines of a petit-bourgeois foundation, found itself squeezed between the parliamentarism from whose "hegemony" it had freed itself and outright coup-mongering. Trying to organize its social uprising within this "narrow" and "shifting" space by developing extraordinary "techniques" and "instruments," it acquired for itself a new "identity" — one that would also, in the end, "bring about its own demise." With an "astonishing" foresight, it chose the one and only method that would "astonish everyone": "empiricism"; those with a good "empirical record" "leapt to the front." "Intellectual revolutionism," "intellectual leadership," and the "naivety" and "gentility" that came with them lost some of their popularity and importance; people began dealing directly with the realities behind issues, and theory began to be looked down upon. Almost the following view prevailed: "A revolutionary is one who wishes to fight for the revolution." Anyone who preferred debate was weak and flawed.

(…) As 1968 began, the seeming "unity" of Turkey's socialist movement was also coming to an end. The seeds of division had been sown during the 1951 arrests; in the years that followed, they were "fed" by the "conflicts" within the TKP, and readied for "birth." When, in '68, the whole globe was electrified by a wave of excitement and revolution drew near enough, everywhere, to be "touched by hand," "it," too, "opened its eyes" to the world. The MDD line — one of the branches of this division — and its leader, Mihri Belli, "took under their guidance" the socialist youth who were then arming themselves with this new "identity."245

Erol Korkmaz, Kafa Tutan Günler

1968 is the year in which reaction organized itself in Turkey, in which fascist commando camps rapidly multiplied, and in which rightist, fascist attacks were at their most intense. January 1968: The "Eastern Night" and the Attack

On 25 January 1968, the FKF Istanbul Secretariat organized the "Eastern Night" at the Spor ve Sergi Sarayı, together with "the local student associations of the Kurdish provinces" — an event that journalist and writer Ragıp Zarakolu, who was himself among the young people present that night, would years later describe as "the magnificent meeting of socialist youth and Kurdish youth."247 Later in the evening, rightist youth attacked the event with bottles of ammonia and acid in hand.

Rightist students, gathered at the Milli Türk Talebe Birliği under the leadership of two "fedai" known for storming meetings, had completed their preparations: to recognize one another, they would wear a visible safety pin on their lapel,248 and use the word "Operation" as their password. Once one of them shouted "Down with the communists," they would launch their attack with clubs, pistols, and bottles filled with acid and ammonia.

About a hundred and fifty militants, scattered among the audience, began setting off Molotov cocktails and smashing the bottles they had brought while the Diyarbakır team was performing a folk dance. A dense smell and cloud of nitric acid and ammonia filled the air. Meanwhile, stones thrown from outside shattered the hall's windows. One of the well-known "fedai" was caught by the young people who had organized the event. In response, he drew his pistol and fired twice, wounding two young people. Another attacker used a knuckle-duster to wound a young man in the eye.

June 1968: Occupations at the Universities

The occupations that began in June 1968 are regarded as the actions that led an entire generation in Turkey to be called the "'68 Generation."274 These occupations first broke out at Ankara's Faculty of Language, History and Geography, and then spread to Istanbul University. In the occupations in Istanbul, the name "Deniz Gezmiş" became increasingly well known. These occupations later spread to faculties and higher schools across many parts of Turkey.

As the crowd returned to the İTÜ dormitory after the protest demonstration, a disagreement broke out between TİP-influenced FKF members and youth belonging to the Devrimci Öğrenci Birliği (DÖB), led by Deniz Gezmiş. Although the FKF members wanted the march to end, the DÖB youth set off marching toward Dolmabahçe, set fire to the American soldiers' liaison offices on the quay, and threw the American soldiers they caught into the sea. While all these protest actions were underway, the police were not the only ones leftist youth found themselves face to face with. As if to give substance to Mehmet Şevket Eygi's words in his column of 18 July — "We must prepare feverishly. All anticommunist university students must be ready to struggle against the communists. Also, in Konya — Vedat Demircioğlu's hometown — before a rally titled "Condemning American Imperialism," which leftist students, together with representatives of TÖS, TÖDMEF, and the FKF, wanted to hold, a nationalist-conservative group that got wind of the rally destroyed the Teachers' Association, TİP's Konya Provincial Center, and printing houses, bookstores, newspaper offices, entertainment venues, and casinos.279

Another development that came to light during all these actions was the division within the youth itself. Within the FKF and the youth organizations at universities, the question of whether TİP supporters or those adopting the "National Democratic Revolution" thesis would hold sway now showed itself as a matter of internal struggle and a division of power. Among the students coming to the fore, Deniz Gezmiş, and, later, Mahir Çayan and İbrahim Kaypakkaya — names that would become widely known — sided with the MDD. Although TİP managed, at its general executive board meeting of 14 July 1968,280 to secure the FKF leadership for a student from its own circle, this state of affairs would not last long, and TİP's influence within the youth movement would steadily decline from this period onward.

August 1968: "Commandos Against the Communists"

August was a month in which the tension between the right and the left increased, and the commando camps took their place on the country's agenda. On 18 August, Türkeş made the following statement: "At the places called commando camps, our youth branches engage in various sporting and cultural activities. We have patriotic, nationalist children who will speak to them in the language they understand. This is why we are raising our young people to be fighters."282

The events of August show that tension was steadily rising. On 4 August, rightists organized an "anti-communism" rally in Istanbul. On 11 August, 25 foreign students who had come for a "universal peace festival," organized by student organizations but banned by the Ministry of the Interior, were deported. On 16 August, MTTB-affiliated youth left a black wreath in front of TİP's offices, arguing that the party should be shut down. On 17 August, the governorship of Van refused to permit the "Anatolian Caravan," which leftist youth had wanted to organize.

The Soviet Union and the countries of the Warsaw Pact invaded Czechoslovakia on 21 August. This invasion led to differing stances within the Turkish left itself. October–November 1968: Boycotts and the "Mustafa Kemal March"

The occupations held in June over demands for reform turned into boycotts in October and November. Boycotts came onto the agenda at universities in Ankara in October, and in Istanbul in November. Because of these boycotts, university senates convened and took up the students' demands. The "Revolutionary Education Council," convened on 4–8 September, and the series of actions launched at the Faculty of Political Science under the leadership of the SBF Sosyalist Fikir Kulübü, aimed at "Education for Revolution," show that students' demands regarding instruction were increasingly being voiced together with the word "revolution." Murat Cahit Kocaoğlu, Chairman of the SBFÖD and a member of the Sosyalist Fikir Kulübü, in a statement on the actions at the SBF, said: "(…) Youth wishing to bring solutions to problems must know its fundamental aim. This aim is the power of the working class. In this way, youth has taken on an extremely important, revolutionary duty: to help the working class come to power, and to accelerate this process."294

The "Mustafa Kemal March," beginning in Samsun and ending in Ankara, was carried out within this same atmosphere. This march, which began on 30 October, came onto the agenda at Dev-Güç's proposal, and the TMGT, DÖB, AÜTB, ODTÜ ÖB, TÖDMF, 27 Mayıs Devrim Derneği, HÜ ÖB, Ankara Yüksek Okullar Talebe Birliği, and Aşıklar Derneği decided to take part in the march. November 1968: The Arrival of "Vietnam's Butcher" Komer, and "Counter-Violence"

Reactions began as soon as it was announced that Robert William Komer, known as "Vietnam's butcher," had been appointed America's new ambassador to Ankara. On 27 November, the FKF Chairman and the SBF ÖD Chairman launched a campaign declaring Komer "persona non grata." The SBF ÖD published a pamphlet announcing the campaign, and called on President Sunay not to accept the letter of credence Komer would present.

On 28 November, the day Komer was to arrive, some of the students who went to Atatürk Airport in Yeşilköy, Istanbul, to protest — Deniz Gezmiş and Cihan Alptekin among them — were taken into custody. That same day, some 1,500 students affiliated with ODTÜ and Ankara University went to Esenboğa Airport in Ankara and organized a protest demonstration. Komer, landing at a different airport, avoided encountering the protesting students.

İÜİF Fikir Kulübü Chairman Ragıp Zarakolu and İÜİF TC Deputy Chairman Ahmet Özsoy published a declaration titled "Fellow University Student" on 29 November, announcing that they were protesting Komer's arrival. The declaration's opening lines read as follows:296

Another blow has been struck against our national independence and honor. Vietnam's butcher, Robert Komer, has been appointed American ambassador to Turkey. FKF Chairman Zülküf Şahin, together with SBF ÖD Chairman Murat Cahit Kocaoğlu and the chairmen of the ODTÜ Mechanical, Electrical, and Administrative Sciences Student Associations, held a joint press conference on 3 December and dwelt on the possible reasons behind Komer's arrival:

The United States' new ambassador to Ankara, Robert W. Komer, is a CIA agent. Now that the United States has shifted the whole of its weight onto the Middle East, those who govern our country need to think very carefully about the reasons behind the arrival of a man notorious for the savagery of imperialism.

Actions of 1969

From "Student Youth" to Youth Aiming at a "Proletarian Organization"

1968 had been a year in which leftist youth raised their struggle through boycotts and occupations, and increasingly broke away from TİP's circle to organize around the MDD thesis. The youth organization had accelerated its internal split into "socialist revolutionaries" (those organized around TİP) and "democratic revolutionaries." 1969 would be the year in which the MDD-ists became unquestionably dominant. At the FKF's 3rd Congress, held on 4–5 January 1969, the MDD-ists won most of the seats on the General Executive Board. At the first General Executive Board meeting, held on 12 January, organizational leadership, apart from the Istanbul Secretariat, fell into the hands of the MDD-ists, and Yusuf Küpeli — a well-known figure among MDD-ist youth, who would later become one of the founders of the THKP-C alongside Mahir Çayan — was elected Chairman.306

"Bulletin No. 1," published by the FKF Head Office Executive Board on 20 January, is sufficiently explanatory as to youth's orientation:307

At its first meeting, held on 12 January 1969, the Central Executive Board agreed on the view that the dominant contradiction in Turkey lies between American imperialism, its collaborators, and feudal remnants on one side, and the proletariat and other nationalist classes and strata on the other. It was likewise agreed that a contradiction also exists between the proletariat and the other nationalist classes and strata, but that this is a weak contradiction that need not be resolved first, alongside the other. In this situation, the proletariat — the class with the greatest revolutionary potential, being the most productive and most oppressed class — must, aware of its ultimate goal, the goal of socialism, bring together all the other nationalist classes and strata on the path to the preliminary stage of clearing out American Imperialism, its collaborators, and feudal remnants, and fulfill its historic leadership role.

The Step Ahead

For the proletariat to be able to fully carry out this leadership duty, all proletarian elements must be united within a single organization, given the doctrine of scientific socialism in depth, and prepared for this leadership. What must be done is to carry forward a two-pronged course of action together. While forging organic ties with all nationalist classes and strata, setting their revolutionary potential against American colonialism into motion and thereby widening the front, it is also necessary to move rapidly toward completing the proletarian organization. Without doubt, the force union that is being sought, under the proletarian organization's leadership, among the classes and strata disturbed by American colonialism, will, in the broadest sense, emerge during the period when the hot war is at its fiercest. Bearing all this in mind, the proletarian organization must, on the path ahead — the stage of national democratic revolution, that is, the stage of clearing out American colonialism, its collaborators, and feudal remnants — embark on systematic revolutionary action that will gather the nationalist forces together. In short, while action is taken, on the strategic plan, for the stage of national democratic revolution, the work of socialist organization and consciousness-raising will also be carried forward rapidly. To think otherwise — to imagine we are already at the stage of socialist revolution and drive the proletariat into battle alone — is equivalent to sending, to their deaths, the very cadres meant to fulfill the leadership duty for national democratic revolution and, in the end, build socialism. To think this way is adventurism, and, knowingly or not, betrayal of the socialist struggle.

As Youth

As we, the youth wing of this struggle, strengthen our own organization, we can also help pioneer the forging of organic ties between the nationalist forces and the proletarian revolutionaries. Indeed, this unity has already naturally begun to be secured against the handful of degenerate commandos who, knowingly or not, serve as tools of American colonialism. The conscious fascists in the service of American colonialism, having nothing to say to the people, have manufactured a commando myth, gathering around this myth the poor sons of the people whose minds have been washed by a secondary education, young people who cannot find work because of the flaws in the system, and traditionalist groups, and trying to use them against revolutionary forces. The poor sons of the people, unaware of what is happening, must come to their senses. They must understand that they are shooting themselves, locking themselves into darkness. Their ignorance will not, one day, be excuse enough for their forgiveness.

As for the protests carried out against America, these are the joint actions of all of us. What must be done now is to halt the chain of actions, make the most of the fruits of the incident, and make the incident the property of the broad masses of the people. What America wants, however, is to draw the proletarian revolutionaries into a chain of terrorist actions cut off from the people, and thereby round them up before they have strengthened their own organization or forged organic ties with the other nationalist forces. As a result, the socialist and anti-imperialist movement would be set back, and the liberation of our people would be delayed. For now, they have not succeeded in this, and the incident has begun to become the property of the broad masses. In the anger produced by this failure, they prepared a foolish plan and planted a harmless bomb at their own supply center. The period we are in is, for socialists, a period of strategic organization. In this period, every terroristic act cut off from the people is adventurism, and harms the movement. We must take care to ensure, as far as possible, that the actions we take are mass actions. Socialist militants must bear this in mind and must not undertake action on their own, cut off from the center's control.

Student youth were now speaking of "uniting all proletarian elements within a single organization," of "the proletarian organization embarking on systematic revolutionary action that will gather the nationalist forces together," of "leadership." Within only a few years, the channel they had entered would lead them to the founding of the Türkiye Halk Kurtuluş Partisi-Cephesi (THKP-C), the Türkiye Halk Kurtuluş Ordusu (THKO), the Türkiye İşçi Köylü Komünist Ordusu (TİKKO), and the Türkiye İhtilalci İşçi Köylü Partisi (TİİKP). In this way, the "proletarian organization," the "vanguard" that would carry out "revolutionary actions," would be created.

The students had no visible instrument, whether organizational or theoretical, capable of producing radical change; the underground was not yet known; and yet they rose up regardless; to an outside observer, this was unexpected conduct — "a complete shock."

Students, moving toward creating new political paths by forging connections between student conditions and international issues, tried to "reconcile," with the Marxist model, the idea of "making revolution through a vanguard" that had been born with the Cuban Revolution and was beginning to "warm" a significant part of the world, and set out to "prove their mettle" against the loose Orthodox clustering led by TİP. 1969: Youth in the Rising Struggle of Workers and Peasants

1969 was a year in which workers' and peasants' actions increased. Leftist youth, too, took part in this movement, as supporters of these actions and, in places, as one of their organizers. This movement, rising in 1969, would reach its peak within a year, with the actions of 15–16 June 1970.

1969's first workers' action took place at the Singer factory. On 10 January, workers occupied the Singer factory in Kartal. On 11 January, police intervened in the occupation with tear gas and smoke bombs. Following a resistance that lasted 5 hours, 17 people were wounded and 118 workers were taken into custody. Following the occupation, DİSK issued a statement calling on the "helpless" Prime Minister Demirel to resign. The FKF Istanbul Secretariat and the İTÜ Öğrenci Birliği also published a joint declaration, stating, "Your fellow students stand by your resistance," and adding the observation: "The Turkish worker has now come to understand that this system of exploitation must come to an end."309

One of 1969's most massive workers' actions was the strike by Sümerbank workers. The Teksif union decided to strike, covering 31,000 workers at 25 workplaces. Although Prime Minister Demirel served as a mediator between the union and the employer, no solution was reached, and the strikes began in the final days of January. Spreading step by step, the strikes came to an end when an agreement was reached on 1 February. The FKF Central Executive Board published a declaration addressed to the striking workers, including the words, "In this just struggle for bread, you must know that we, revolutionary Turkish youth, will fight alongside you to the very end."

1969 also saw new momentum in the peasant movements. Land occupations began. Mass actions were carried out. Leftist youth, too, in order to take part in and help organize these peasant actions, left their universities and scattered across every part of Turkey. Esat Korkmaz assesses these peasant actions as follows:310

With 1969 came peasant movements employing radical and democratic methods of struggle not previously seen. Poor and landless peasants, together with small producers, gathered around demands for fair exchange against large landowners, the usurer-merchants, and the state, and undertook "incendiary" actions. The peasant struggle rapidly became a mass movement; entering into an organic relationship with the opposition classes and strata of society, it took on a political character, causing anti-imperialist demands to find an echo in the countryside as well. Yet, for all their incendiary character, peasant actions never took the form of a direct attack on large landed property or merchant capital: they mostly created de facto situations and occupations aimed at reclaiming, and putting to their own use, "seemingly ownerless" village common lands and treasury lands that landlords had easily seized. Small producers, meanwhile, generally did not embark on a direct attack on property in general, or merchant capital in particular; instead, they expressed their reaction by protesting the government's crop floor-price policies through mass rallies, deferring their debts to the state, and refusing to pay their debts to usurers.

Esat Korkmaz

1969's first peasant actions were carried out in January, by the villagers of Atalan and Göllüce in İzmir-Torbalı. The villagers occupied lands belonging to large landowners. The lands occupied in the village of Atalan belonged to Mesude Evliyazade, Adnan Menderes's aunt. The villagers of Göllüce, meanwhile, began using pastureland belonging to the treasury. As these occupations continued, the FKF and 28 student organizations published a declaration on 4 February, condemning the government for failing to carry out land reform and expressing their support for the peasant actions:311

To Our Peasant Brothers

(…) The land you till is your own property. The exploitative landlords have no right to that land. The land belongs to the one who tills it, who sheds their sweat upon it. We declare that, as Revolutionary Turkish Youth, we stand with you to the very end in this just labor struggle of yours. We condemn the government, which fails to carry out the Land Reform the Constitution demands.

Joint declaration of the FKF and 28 student organizations, 4 February 1969

These land occupations, and the peasants' resistance, could only be suppressed through the intervention of the gendarmerie.

As peasant actions increased in the Aegean, the FKF launched a campaign of activity in the region. Young people, FKF Chairman Yusuf Küpeli among them, traveled to the region, and "Aegean Tobacco Rallies" began to be organized. The first of these, held in Akhisar on 7 February and attended by 4,000–5,000 people, was targeted for intervention by a group of 100 people gathered under the leadership of the Association for the Struggle Against Communism, and brief clashes broke out. During the rally, slogans of "Peasant and Youth, Hand in Hand" were chanted.312 Following the clashes in Akhisar, a rally planned for the next day in Saruhan was abandoned. On 10 February, a rally was held in Ödemiş, İzmir. Yusuf Küpeli, in a piece describing the work carried out in the region, relates that they visited more than 59 villages in and around Ödemiş, and that they also went to the villages of Atalan and Göllüce, where the occupations had taken place.313

1969: The Year Deaths Became Commonplace

Leftist youth's steadily gathering struggle, increasingly oriented toward the goal of "revolution," found its reflection in the organizing of rightist, nationalist-traditionalist youth as well. Backed by the government, these youth organizations sought to build a "wall" against leftist youth. Rightist youth, trained in the commando camps and honed in the Associations for the Struggle Against Communism and similar organizations, increasingly turned to violence as their principal instrument. "Violence" increasingly became the language of youth organizations.

Esat Korkmaz, who was part of leftist youth in this period, assesses those days as follows:314

The struggle for democratization, institutionally and in terms of education, turned the autonomous universities into fortresses of socialist youth. As youth leapt to the fore, the autonomous universities — emerging as centers where nearly every kind of logistics was secured — drew down the "lightning bolts" of political power upon themselves; to break up these fortresses, under socialists' hegemony, the government turned a blind eye to the fascist movement's violent assault on students, and, going further still, prepared the conditions for it. In this way, the struggle took on a state of violence, involving deadly, live firearms, escalating a little further with each passing day. The entry of violence, in this sense, into the methods of struggle was a radical change, going beyond the violence that had, until then, remained within traditional bounds and been carried out mainly with stones and clubs; the compulsion this change created was enveloped by a "taste of war"; conduct became "militarized"; activities related to the fine arts were pushed to the background; naivety disappeared; it was no longer the "burning urgency" of thought but the "destructiveness" of action that came to the fore. The debates over seeking new political paths and organizing were all carried out, and conditioned by, this same atmosphere.

The CKMP, observed to have entered these new conditions of struggle better prepared, thanks to the opportunities the government had created, moved toward a reorganization built directly on military foundations, in order to bring onto the agenda, and impose, a fascist solution from within the existing order; taking on a new identity under the name Milliyetçi Hareket Partisi (MHP), it devoted the whole of its strength and activity to launching attacks on revolutionary youth and the socialist movement, and to training the cadres necessary for this purpose.

Provocations and Agents

1969 was also the year in which provocations were staged. Viewed from today, these provocations recall, on one hand, the beginning of the 6–7 September 1955 events, and, on the other, the "the communists planted a bomb in the mosque" pattern and similar examples we would later witness in the Maraş, Çorum, and Sivas massacres. This draws attention to the power, or powers, behind the scenes — the formation referred to as the "counter-guerrilla," the "deep state," "Gladio" — and its extensions. The first "the communists planted a bomb in the mosque" story came onto the agenda in 1968. On 11 August, a bomb went off in front of the Hisar Mosque in İzmir, where Mehmet Şevket Eygi had come to lead a communal prayer. A day later, newspapers ran the headline "A Bomb Was Set Off in Front of a Mosque in İzmir, Before the 'Suppressing Communism' Prayer."332 That same day, it was reported that Molotov cocktails had also been thrown at the American Information Center, the Nationalist Teachers' Association, and the Association for the Struggle Against Communism. According to the newspaper's report, one of the three people taken into custody had been dismissed from his post as a non-commissioned officer on the grounds of being a communist, and had twice been admitted to a psychiatric hospital; the second had previously been convicted on the grounds of being a "Bulgarian spy." Milliyet noted, however, that rumors of "there's going to be an explosion here" had circulated about 10 minutes before the bomb left at the mosque's ablution fountain went off, raising suspicions that the sabotage might be "the work of a plot." That this incident was a provocation came to light during the judicial process. The person who left the bomb at the mosque's ablution fountain was a "police agent."333

The second "bomb in the mosque" provocation took place on 7 July 1969, in Kayseri. On 7 July, the Türkiye Öğretmenler Sendikası (TÖS) was holding its congress in Kayseri. On the night the congress was to be held, the electricity was cut, and explosions took place at two mosques, an association, and the İmam Hatip Lisesi. Rumors that "the communists bombed the mosque" spread rapidly through the city, and, the following morning, a crowd that had gathered attacked the Alemdar Cinema, where the congress was being held. The cinema was doused in gasoline and set on fire. At the same time, the TİP provincial building, the TÖS clubhouse, the Tok Kitapevi bookstore, and the hotels where the teachers were staying were raided and destroyed. The teachers were rescued by military units that arrived three hours after the attack began. During these events, AP Senator Haydar Dikeçligil and AP Kayseri deputy Mehmet Ateşoğlu were also in Kayseri. Ateşoğlu, in a statement issued after the events, declared that "the sellout, red lackeys of the Muscovite would be struck down like dogs":334

The Turkish nation, exercising its right of self-defense to protect its national existence and its faith, will rise up with the hatred and vengeance of the Muscovite, and will most certainly strike down and destroy the sellout red lackeys of the Muscovite like dogs. It will make them worse off even than Indonesia. In matters of homeland and nation, as our great Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) states in a hadith, "he who shows no mercy shall receive none." The Turkish nation will make this life forbidden to the communists, will make this homeland their grave.

AP Kayseri Deputy Mehmet Ateşoğlu

Following the incident, the FKF, Ankara University, Hacettepe University, ODTÜ, DTCF, SBF, and the Student Associations of the Faculties of Medicine, Agriculture, Pharmacy, Veterinary Medicine, the İktisadi ve Ticari İlimler Akademisi (İTİA), Science, Gazi Eğitim, the Erkek Teknik Yüksek Öğretmen Okulu, and the Basın Yayın Yüksek Okulu, along with the Emperyalizmle Mücadele Derneği, the İşsizlik ve Pahalılıkla Savaş Derneği, and the Hacettepe Temel Bilimler Fakültesi Student Association, jointly published a declaration stating that "the dynamiting incident in Kayseri was staged by reactionaries, and the people were provoked in this manner," and declaring, "In Turkey, reactionary violence invites revolutionary violence":335

The nature of the incident is clear. Reaction — financed by American imperialists and stirred up by collaborator bosses, landlords, usurers, and sheikhs — has embarked on a fresh attempt at another Kanlı Pazar. The state's security forces are, in effect, helping them. The lives and property of citizens working for Atatürk's reforms, for independence and democracy, are no longer secure. As has been true throughout all of history, in Turkey too, reactionary violence invites revolutionary violence. Patriotic revolutionaries, too, will do what the situation demands. They will use every weapon and every means of struggle to secure and protect the Constitution, democracy, and independence in the country. Reaction and its supporters will be destroyed. Turkey will never become Indonesia.

Joint declaration, July 1969

Beyond the provocative actions, the presence of numerous agents who had infiltrated the youth movement would also later come to light. The best known of these is Mahir Kaynak. Mahir Kaynak, who would be revealed, during the Madanoğlu Trial, to be an agent of the National Intelligence Organization (MİT), was, in those years, an assistant at Istanbul University. He held meetings with revolutionary youth and incited them toward violent action with statements such as, "The strength of the revolutionary struggle is weapons. An unarmed struggle cannot succeed. Let us put an end to pacifism."336 Şükran Soner, too, speaks of Kaynak as "a stricter leftist than any of us at the time," and describes how he even recorded her own wedding dinner and reported it as an "organizational meeting," and how he stood at the center of the Madanoğlu trial.337 Besides Kaynak, Muzaffer Köklü, Erdal Gökyüzü, and Eyüp Temeltaş are among some of the others who were used as agents within the youth movement of the period. Köklü, who carried an ODTÜ identity card, was someone who stood out within leftist youth for his fiery stances, and who, in the actions he took part in, worked to steer youth toward aggressive conduct. To protest the Sixth Fleet's arrival, he even declared he would douse himself in gasoline and set himself on fire in Kızılay, and, during the "Mustafa Kemal March" organized from Samsun to Ankara, he tried to tear up the Turkish flag.338 Erdal Gökyüzü, who would later serve as Chief of Police, also attended rallies armed with a switchblade and a firearm and, like Köklü, stood out for his pro-violence stances. Eyüp Temeltaş, an ODTÜ student, was a MİT member. When he was taken into custody on 25 August 1971, Lieutenant General Nurettin Esin, Undersecretary of MİT, wrote a letter to the Ankara Martial Law Prosecutor's Office requesting that his situation not be exposed, and Temeltaş was released two days after this letter was sent.339 Mahmut Erdoğan was a student in ODTÜ's Physics Department. During his trial in 1971, it came to light that he had been "recruited" on 29 May 1970 and had worked as an agent from that date onward, and he was released 8 days after the Director General of Security, Sedat Kirtepe, wrote a letter to the court.340 Another "agent" exposed during the trials was Uğur Sani, a student at the Ankara Faculty of Law.341

The events of 13 February 1969 are an example of these agents' activities and machinations. When Muzaffer Köklü announced that he would set himself on fire in Kızılay to protest the Sixth Fleet's arrival, 38 of the people who gathered in front of the Zafer Monument on 13 February were taken into custody, on the grounds that "they staged an unlawful demonstration, clashed with police, and threw stones at the American supply center, Tuslog." But FKF Secretary-General Mehmet Demir describes what happened that day as follows:342

There was no incident. But the police knew how to create one. As the crowd was being dispersed, young policemen in plain clothes shouted, "Come on, let's do something, the crowd's here." The police, getting ahead of the crowd, dragged [the crowd] toward the American supply center. Those who came out of curiosity to see what would happen were surrounded and caught by uniformed police once they arrived. What's more, there was only one person who threw stones at the American supply center. And that person was a police officer disguised as a shoeshiner. With this incident, the police have entered into an extensive scheme. But we did not fall for it. Even so, many of our friends were arrested as suspects.

FKF Secretary-General Mehmet Demir

When the Sixth Fleet returned to Istanbul's harbors in February, a process had begun in which leftist and rightist youth had started speaking the language of violence, and in which provocateurs were hard at work. On 10 February, the day the Fleet anchored off Dolmabahçe, leftist youth's protest actions began, and the threshold of the process that would evolve into "Kanlı Pazar" had now been reached. Only a few more steps would be needed to cross that threshold, and to enter the process of massacres that Turkey would witness many more times in the years to come.

Chronology Note: The Johnson Letter and İnönü's "New World" Remark

Two developments often recalled one after the other in Turkish public discourse — İnönü's remark, "A new world will be built…" and the Johnson Letter — are, in fact, parts of the same line of political tension, though they are often treated within an incorrect chronological order and causal relationship. İsmet İnönü's remark, "A new world will be built, and Turkey will find its own new place in that world," is often remembered as though it were a direct response to the Johnson Letter; in fact, it was made in an interview he gave to Time magazine in April 1964, roughly a month and a half before the letter, dated 5 June 1964.

That said, both İnönü's statement and the Johnson Letter are markers of the same political tension, and of the Turkey–U.S./NATO crisis that grew up around the Cyprus question. Turkey's mistrust of, and growing discomfort with, its U.S. and NATO allies over Cyprus was the source of İnönü's statement; in June, the decision was taken to intervene militarily in Cyprus, and the Johnson Letter blocked this intervention.

Although the full text of the Johnson Letter only reached the public in 1966, its existence and content were known from 1964 onward, and it marks a turning point in Turkey–U.S. relations as the document that halted Turkey's intervention in Cyprus (Alptekin and Özer, 2020).

Both the Johnson Letter and İnönü's words would take their place in political memory as two significant symbols of the anti-American stance developing in Turkey, one that would grow steadily stronger in the years that followed.
