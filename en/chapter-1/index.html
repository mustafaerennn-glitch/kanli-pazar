Chapter 1

The Political Climate After 27 May 1960

pp. 23–102
27 May 1960, Havadis

Taking a bird's-eye view of Turkey's political life, we can sketch out the following periodization:1

1— The period of Republican People's Party (CHP) rule, running from the 1920s — that is, from the founding of the Republic — to 1946–1950. Setting aside the Progressive Republican Party and Free Republican Party initiatives, this can also be called the single-party period.

2— The multi-party period after 1950. CHP rule came to an end at this point, and the Democrat Party (DP) took its place in parliament and in power with a sweeping electoral majority.

This multi-party period was interrupted three times:

  • 1960–1961 (by the 27 May coup)
  • 1971–1973 (by the 12 March coup)
  • 1980–1983 (by the 12 September coup)

Alongside these three coups, mention should also be made of the 28 February "postmodern coup," the alleged coup plots known as "Sarıkız," "Ayışığı," "Yakamoz," "Eldiven," and "Balyoz," and the coup attempts Talat Aydemir carried out on 22 February 1962 and on 20–21 May 1963. Because the "military intervention" that came to be known as "28 February," following decisions taken at a National Security Council meeting on 28 February 1997, did not lead to the closure of parliament, it does not rank as a fourth interruption among these; even so, it is well known that 28 February caused the resignation of Necmettin Erbakan, then prime minister in the Welfare Party (RP)–True Path Party (DYP) coalition government, and that the government ultimately collapsed. Also still fresh in memory is the description, by then Deputy Chief of the General Staff General Çevik Bir, of the tanks driven through Sincan — one of the first steps in this "intervention" — as "giving democracy a balance adjustment."2 The alleged coup plots known as "Sarıkız," "Ayışığı," "Yakamoz," "Eldiven," and "Balyoz" surfaced in 2003–2004, on claims that they had been planned against the then Justice and Development Party government, and became the subject of court cases.3

On 27 May 1960, the army put an end to ten years of DP rule, seized control of the government, and dissolved parliament. Together with the later 28 February "postmodern military intervention," the alleged "Sarıkız, Ayışığı, Yakamoz, Eldiven, Balyoz" coup plots, and Talat Aydemir's coup attempts, this "military intervention" became one of the most debated of Turkey's coups. The "twin twelves" (the 12 March 1971 and 12 September 1980 coups) are rarely debated as "coups" at all — indeed, they can be described as "military fascist juntas" — whereas 27 May can be characterized as a "revolution" or "political revolution,"4 and 28 February, unable to fit the classic mold of a "coup," can be called "a coup, but a postmodern one."5

Following 27 May, elections were held on 15 October 1961, and on 20 November 1961 a CHP–AP (Justice Party) coalition was formed. In the interval between these dates, the country remained under the administration of the National Unity Committee — that is, of the army.

The Army After 27 May

The National Unity Committee (MBK)

The 27 May coup was carried out by a group of colonels and senior officers. The fact that it was carried out against the wishes of the generals is one feature that distinguishes it from the coups that would follow. 27 May was directed against the military hierarchy just as much as against DP rule.6

Thirty-eight of the officers who carried out the 27 May coup took power under the name National Unity Committee (MBK). The MBK held power until 25 October 1961. Immediately after the coup, however, purges took place within the army that also affected the MBK itself — a sign that there was disagreement and a power struggle within the military as well.

The MBK's first significant actions were the following:

1— The Yassıada Trials

The trials of DP members arrested after 27 May were held on Yassıada, an island in the middle of the Sea of Marmara that could be described as sterile [i.e. isolated]. The hearings, which began on 14 October 1960, ended on 15 September 1961. The death penalty was sought for 288 of the 592 "defendants." Fifteen were sentenced to death, 31 to life imprisonment, 418 received various other sentences, and 123 were acquitted. The MBK approved only 3 of the 15 death sentences. Fatin Rüştü Zorlu and Hasan Polatkan were executed on 16 September, and Adnan Menderes on 17 September.

2— The Purges at the Universities: The "147s" Affair

In October 1960, the MBK removed 147 faculty members and assistants from their university posts. This purge came to be known as the "147s affair," and those purged as "the 147s."7 In its wake, many rectors and faculty members resigned in protest. Prof. Mina Urgan, who was herself among those removed, described the episode as the administration "feinting right while striking left."

29 October 1960, Cumhuriyet

3— The Attempt to Suppress Kurdish Opposition and the "Sivas Camp"

One of the targets of the 27 May coup was possible Kurdish opposition. Within four days of the coup, 485 people — ranging in age from 14 to 70, and known either as Kurdish notables or as members of prominent Kurdish tribes — were taken into custody. A special camp was set up for them within the military garrison of the 5th Recruit Training Brigade in Kabakyazı, Sivas. The 485 were held there, at this "Sivas Camp," for 90 days. At the end of this forced ninety-day stay, 55 of them were sent into exile.

4— Purges Within the Army: The Eminsu, the Fourteen, Talat Aydemir, and the Armed Forces Union

One of the MBK's first actions was to carry out purges within the army itself. The first purge took place on 3 August 1960. Citing Law No. 42, which allowed for the retirement of officers who had completed 25 years of active service, the MBK administration retired 235 generals and admirals along with nearly 5,000 officers. These purged officers would later come to be known as the Retired Revolutionary Officers (Eminsu). This purge served, through restructuring, to legitimize the MBK's authority over the armed forces.8

Along with the Eminsu, General Ragıp Gümüşpala — whom the MBK had earlier appointed Chief of the General Staff — was also purged. Gümüşpala, retired on 2 August 1960, would later go on to found the AP.

The second purge within the army took place on 13 November 1960. Those purged became known as the "Fourteen." Within the 38-member MBK, two distinct tendencies had emerged immediately after the coup. Members of the first — who might be called the "extremists"9 or the "radical wing"10 — wanted to continue governing the country under military rule even after the coup succeeded.11 Instead of a parliament made up of parties, they envisioned establishing a single national assembly that could work in cooperation with representatives of various professional organizations yet to be formed.12

14 November 1960, Cumhuriyet

Twenty-one of the MBK's 38 officers belonged to this group. Those in the second tendency — who might be called the "moderates"13 — held, under the influence of İsmet İnönü, that elections should be held and power transferred to parliament as soon as the necessary legal and political arrangements had been made.14 It is an interesting detail that the "extremists" had an average age of 37.2, while the "moderates" had an average age of 45.3.15 Taken together with the fact that everyone who carried out this particular coup came from the relatively lower ranks of the army, this suggests that the younger coup-makers held the more extreme demands.

15 November 1960, Cumhuriyet

The most prominent figure among the "extremists" was Alpaslan Türkeş, who had stood trial in the 1940s in the "Turanism"16 cases. Türkeş is known as the architect of the "extremists'" designs.

Within days of being removed from the MBK and purged from the army, these 14 officers were given advisory status and sent into exile at Turkish embassies abroad.

The Armed Forces Union (SKB)

Madanoğlu, who had carried out the purge of the Fourteen, would himself see his own power curtailed not long after. This was accomplished by a structure that would emerge within the army and, despite the MBK, run the government from behind the scenes: the Armed Forces Union (SKB). This structure demonstrated itself and its strength when the MBK sought to remove General İrfan Tansel from his post, issuing an ultimatum to the General Staff on 6 June 1961. Alongside its demand that Tansel be reinstated as commander of the Air Force, the ultimatum also included a list of military officials whose retirement or reassignment was demanded. Madanoğlu himself appeared on this list, with the demand that he be removed from the Ankara Martial Law Command. The ultimatum further demanded that the MBK no longer interfere in promotions and purges within the army. In the end, the MBK was forced to withdraw its decision to remove Tansel, while Madanoğlu, on 6 June, resigned both his MBK membership and his post as Ankara Martial Law Commander. In this way, the new center of power holding the reins of government in the country became clear: the Armed Forces Union.18

But the SKB, too, was not an internally unified structure. The MBK "was an organization born of the hasty coming-together of various secret committees that had emerged within the army at different times," while the SKB, in turn, arose out of the senior cadres of these groups within the MBK operating among themselves like a kind of council.19 The figure to whom all the groups within the SKB ultimately deferred as leader was the Chief of the General Staff, Cevdet Sunay.20

One of the groups within the SKB was "the colonels' junta that emerged in Ankara under the dominance of Colonel Talat Aydemir and his associates — Aydemir having been among the original core group that organized 27 May, but excluded from the MBK because he was on assignment in Korea at the time of the action."21

Talat Aydemir and the Colonels' Junta

Aydemir was born in Bilecik in 1917. He graduated from the Military Academy in 1939 and the War College in 1954, was posted to Korea in 1959, and remained there until June 1960. Despite his absence from Turkey, however, he took part in the preparations for 27 May. After returning home, he was appointed, while holding the rank of staff colonel, as commander of the Military Academy by the MBK. It was after this that the developments that would bring Aydemir's name to prominence began. Following 27 May, Aydemir would go on to make two further coup attempts.

After 27 May, the share of the vote won by the AP, and its participation in government, caused unease in many quarters within the army. This unease gave rise to the idea that the mission of 27 May remained unfinished, and that the military's guiding role needed to continue, through a new intervention, until matters were set on the right track. For this reason, the period is spoken of in terms of "juntas" — juntas that certain factions within the army were preparing. It was not a single "junta" but several. The one that stood out, and that was actually put into action, was the "Colonels' Junta" led by Talat Aydemir.

Because of the growing unease and mounting coup attempts within the army, İsmet İnönü felt compelled, in a radio address in January 1962, to declare that "I will take part in no movement against democracy — on the contrary, I will fight against it."22 Again at İnönü's initiative, the Chief of the General Staff, Sunay, urgently convened the Expanded Command Council. At this meeting, held on 19 January 1962, those within the army who wanted intervention and those who did not would have it out. Seventy-two commanders gathered at General Staff Headquarters. "They had split into two camps: on one side, Talat Aydemir and the colonels demanding intervention… on the other, Sunay and the generals opposing it…"23 At this meeting, Aydemir told the generals and the Chief of the General Staff, in effect, that they should take the lead in a coup, saying, "I am one hundred percent certain there will be a second revolution in this country. A revolution carried out through the hierarchy would do the least harm." He did not stop there, adding a veiled threat: "I am warning you. There is a fracture within the army. Pressure from the lower ranks is growing." Talat Turhan, then head of the private secretariat of the Minister of National Defense, describes this meeting and the mood among the colonels as follows:

"It was an extremely harsh speech — the kind of thing that simply wouldn't happen in the military under normal circumstances. On one side, the Chief of the General Staff and the force commanders, the generals; facing them, a colonel leveling the harshest criticism, and no one able to say a word in response. Why? Because, along that particular line, the colonel was in the right. This wasn't a matter of being for or against democracy. Given the circumstances that had brought things to this point, there was a shared understanding. And within that shared understanding, there was a particular stance: power must not be handed to the counter-revolutionary. Now Sunay, for his part, was pushing the position that it should be handed over — not because he was for democracy, but because he now saw his own interest lying there…"

Talat Turhan, then head of the private secretariat of the Minister of National Defense24

Aydemir's views were clear. In response, Sunay convened a new meeting with the leading commanders on 18 February, found that "the major factions" stood with him, and decided to purge Aydemir and those around him.25 22 February was chosen as the day of the purge. Aydemir and his circle, meanwhile, gathered at Balmumcu on 9 February and decided to seize power within twenty days, having also won over the command echelon and the Air Force. To this end, a written document that would become known as the "9 February Protocol" was signed by the 54 officers present at the meeting.26

The purge plan called for the officers leading the coup attempt to be removed from their posts and reassigned to the East. The reassignment orders were delivered to Aydemir on 21 February, and the coup attempt began the following day.

"What set in motion Aydemir's action on the evening of 22 February 1962 — mobilizing the entire student body of the Military Academy and, with the support of other units, moving to seize control of Ankara — was Sunay's purge plan. Aydemir was so confident that events would unfold as he expected that, having taken İnönü and members of the government captive at Çankaya Palace, he even allowed them to be released. Yet not long after setting his action in motion, Aydemir would find himself alone, as the other senior factions broke away from him one by one. Toward morning, the rebel colonel yielded to Prime Minister İnönü, who announced that those who surrendered without bloodshed would be pardoned and set free. İnönü, even in the eyes of the AP, appeared in that moment as the hero who had saved democracy. Parliament would rise to greet him the following day. Aydemir and his cadres, meanwhile, now stood outside the machinery of the state."

Ali Gevgilili, Yükseliş ve Düşüş (1987)27
Son Havadis — Coup Attempt

Eight thousand soldiers took part in this coup attempt, 500 of them officers. In the end, 69 officers, Aydemir among them, were forced into retirement, and some generals were replaced. But Aydemir set to work on a new attempt from the very first day.

The second coup attempt took place on 20–21 May 1963. Reports circulated at the time that Aydemir had held talks with, and joined forces with, the Fourteen, who had by then returned to the country. In this coup attempt, however, Aydemir would prove "more alone than ever." "According to rumor, even Türkeş — one of the Fourteen who would for a time be tried alongside him — was among those who had abandoned him long before."28

During the events, 1 air force colonel, 1 major, 2 Military Academy cadets, and 4 privates lost their lives, and 26 people were wounded, including senior generals and officers. Following this coup attempt, the entire student body of the Military Academy was expelled, and charges were brought against 1,439 people, including Aydemir. After the trials, Aydemir and six of his associates (Fethi Gürcan, Erol Dinçer, İlhan Baş, Cevat Kırca, Osman Deniz, and Ahmet Güçal) were sentenced to death; 30 people to life imprisonment; and 33 people to prison terms ranging from 3 months to 15 years. Thirty-eight people, including some former officers among the Fourteen, were acquitted. Of the military cadets arrested at the Military Academy, 1,293 were acquitted, while 91 received 3-month sentences and 75 received 4-year sentences. Of those sentenced to death, Major Gürcan was executed on 26 June 1964, and Aydemir on 5 July 1964.29

Although the unrest among the army's lower ranks appeared to have been suppressed by these sentences and executions, it would continue to simmer beneath the surface and would manifest itself again in 1971, in the form of two separate coup attempts. In 1971, there were two coup preparations underway: one, characterized as "leftist," known as "9 March," and the other, characterized as "rightist," known as "12 March." It was the 12 March faction that carried out the coup.30

Governments from 27 May to 12 March

Five governments were formed between 27 May and 12 March. On average, one might say a government changed roughly every two years. But even this does not give the full picture. In the first five years after 27 May, the government changed almost every year, and four separate coalition governments were formed. This alone is enough to show the political turbulence and tension of the period. These five governments were as follows:

1— 20 November 1961 – 1 June 1962 — CHP–AP coalition (First İnönü Coalition)

2— 25 June 1962 – 2 December 1963 — CHP–YTP (New Turkey Party)–CKMP (Republican Peasants' Nation Party) coalition (Second İnönü Coalition)

3— 25 December 1963 – 13 February 1965 — Third Coalition period. The CHP formed the government with the participation of independents, while the YTP provided outside support.

4— 20 February 1965 – 10 October 1965 — Fourth Coalition period. An AP–YTP–CKMP coalition headed by Suat Hayri Ürgüplü. The deputy prime minister was Süleyman Demirel, the AP's chairman, who was not yet a member of parliament.

5— 27 October 1965 – 12 March 1971 — The AP government period, under Prime Minister Süleyman Demirel.

27 May was carried out to end DP rule. Yet in every election held after 27 May, one of the two strongest parties was the AP, seen as the DP's successor. What is more, the AP, together with the other right-wing parties in parliament, was in a position to form a government from the very first election onward. Seen from the military's side, the intervention had failed — it had not worked. The first three coalitions formed after the military intervention came about, in effect, at the army's own prodding. In an arithmetic dominated by the right, every option was tried in an effort to form CHP-led coalitions, but this succeeded only three times. For this reason, the fourth coalition period, from which the CHP was absent, is also seen as marking the beginning of the end of the "transition to civilian rule" — the point at which the army's cold shoulder toward the AP came to an end.31 This assessment, however, does not mean that this process unfolded without difficulty.

The first reflection of this shock within the army was the 21 October Protocol. Ten senior generals and admirals, along with 28 colonels, signed a document that would come to be known as the 21 October Protocol and delivered it to the Chief of the General Staff, Cevdet Sunay. The aim of the protocol was to neutralize, through an intervention to be carried out no later than 25 October, the parliament due to open on 26 October. In other words, parliament was to be shut down before it had even opened! It was only through İsmet İnönü's open declaration that he would stand against any new intervention, and his role as an intermediary, that parliament was able to open.34 To this end, party leaders and commanders met at the Presidential Residence, where negotiations took place. The commanders had four demands:

1— Gürsel's election as president would be made certain

2— The Yassıada convicts would not be pardoned

3— The Eminsu would not be readmitted to the army

4— The 147s would not return to the universities

Parties in the Grand National Assembly After 27 May

As the table above shows, four parties took part in the first election held after 27 May. Of these, the CHP, led by İnönü, and the CKMP, chaired by Osman Bölükbaşı, were the only two parties that survived from before 27 May. The AP and the YTP were both founded in 1961. One more party was founded in 1961: the Turkish Labor Party (TİP).

The Justice Party

It was founded on 11 February 1961. It is known as the DP's successor. Because the DP's leading cadres had been neutralized and stripped of their political rights, it was people from the lower ranks of the party hierarchy who contributed to the founding of the AP. It would be wrong, however, to say that the AP's founders were entirely former DP members; cadres with no DP background were also involved in its founding.

"At its founding stage, the Justice Party lived out both of these possibilities at once. In this way, a door to political struggle and a climb toward power was opened even to the most distant relatives of the DP members being tried on Yassıada. Through this door would pass grieving spouses, and ambitious, resentful children."

Ali Gevgilili, Yükseliş ve Düşüş (1987)36

The AP's first chairman, though not himself a DP member, was another figure who had suffered from 27 May — a soldier, and no ordinary one: a general who, while serving as the first Chief of the General Staff after 27 May, was removed from his post during the purge carried out by the MBK: Ragıp Gümüşpala. Most of the retired officers purged from the army and known as the Eminsu also gave Gümüşpala their support.

The New Turkey Party

A second party on the "right," alongside the AP, was the New Turkey Party, founded in February 1961. Although, just as with the AP, former DP members took part in its founding, the YTP was a party that underscored certain differences setting it apart from the DP.

"There, too, were former DP members. And yet Ekrem Alican, who would go on to lead the YTP, along with Prof. Aydın Yalçın, Yusuf Azizoğlu, and others like them, were people from the liberal wing who, past a certain point, found themselves at odds with the DP's outlook. In keeping with the name they had chosen, they aimed, in this respect, to bring Turkey together along new lines. The feelings of resentment and revenge held by Democrat and Justice Party members were simply not a factor for them."

Ali Gevgilili, Yükseliş ve Düşüş (1987)37

The Turkish Labor Party

A third party founded in February 1961 was the Turkish Labor Party. It was founded by 12 trade unionists.38 In 1962, in order to bring the working class together with intellectuals, a call was issued inviting intellectuals to join the party; in response, many intellectuals joined, among them Mehmet Ali Aybar, Behice Boran, Adnan Cemgil, Nazife Cemgil, Cemal Hakkı Selek, and Fethi Naci. On the founders' recommendation, Assoc. Prof. Dr. Mehmet Ali Aybar was made the party's chairman.

TİP was unable to take part in the first general election held after 27 May. In the 1963 local elections, it was able to run in 9 provinces and 31 districts, winning more than 35,000 votes, 20,000 of them in Istanbul. TİP secured its first representation in parliament in February 1963, when independent senator Niyazi Ağırnaslı joined the party.39 Following Ağırnaslı, appointed senator Esat Çağa also joined TİP.40 In the 1965 election, running in 51 provinces, it won 2.83 percent of the vote and sent 15 deputies to parliament. In the 1966 partial Senate elections, it won a Senate seat as well with a vote share of 3.79 percent, bringing its total representation in parliament to 16. In the 1969 general election, although it won 2.56 percent of the vote, it was able to elect only two deputies because the electoral law had been changed.41

Aybar resigned as chairman on 16 November 1969. After his resignation, the chairmanship was held first by Mehmet Ali Aslan and then by Şaban Yıldız. At TİP's Fourth Grand Congress, held in October 1970, Behice Boran was elected chairman. Following the 12 March coup, the party was shut down by decision of the Constitutional Court on 20 July 1971. The main TİP trial concluded on 17 October 1972, with the party's leaders given heavy prison sentences.

The Republican Peasants' Nation Party – The Nationalist Movement Party

The CKMP was founded on 16 October 1958 through the merger of the Republican Nation Party (CMP) and the Turkish Peasants' Party. Osman Bölükbaşı, previously chairman of the CMP, was elected chairman of the CKMP as well, and led the party until his resignation in 1962. Bölükbaşı resigned as party chairman because the CKMP had joined İnönü's Second Coalition government. After Bölükbaşı, the party was led in turn by Ahmet Tahtakılıç, Ahmet Oğuz, and Alpaslan Türkeş.

Türkeş began to gain influence within the CKMP starting in 1963. The exiled Fourteen began returning to the country from 1963 onward, and Türkeş was among them. After returning, he joined the CKMP at its congress of 22–23 February 1964, together with several other members of the Fourteen (Dündar Taşer, Ahmet Er, Muzaffer Özdağ, Rıfat Baykal, Mustafa Kaplan…). Less than a year after joining, in 1965, he was elected party chairman and steered the party according to his own views. That same year, he was elected as a deputy from Ankara.42

The youth organization of the CKMP (MHP) was the structure known as the "Ülkü Ocakları" (Idealist Hearths). Alongside it, however, the Young Idealists Organization, whose organizing began in 1967, was also for a time among the party's youth organizations. The first Ülkü Ocakları opened in Ankara in 1966. Following the Ülkü Ocakları established at the Ankara Faculty of Law, the Faculty of Language, History and Geography, and the Faculty of Agriculture — which announced their founding on 18 March 1966 — these hearths spread more widely in 1968. On 15 March 1969, in opposition to the leftist youth's Federation of Idea Clubs, the Ülkü Ocakları Birliği [Idealist Hearths Union] was formed as an umbrella organization for the Ülkü Ocakları. Namık Kemal Zeybek, chairman of the CKMP Youth Branches; Dündar Taşer, CKMP deputy chairman; and Muzaffer Özdağ, CKMP deputy secretary-general, all played major roles in the founding and spread of the Ülkü Ocakları.43 Ahmet Karabacak, a leading MHP figure of the period, describes the founding of the Ülkü Ocakları and the Young Idealists Organization as follows:44

The CKMP took the name Nationalist Movement Party (MHP) at its congress held in Adana on 6–8 February 1969. Along with the name, the party's emblem also changed: the scales that had previously served as its emblem gave way to the three crescents. From 1968 onward, the party's name became associated with the "Commando Camps" it established. The "Commando Camps" deserve close attention, as they were among the important actors and parties responsible for this period.

The Commando Camps withdrew from Turkey's political stage in the early 1970s. Öznur states that these camps never reopened after the 12 March coup. There is, however, information suggesting the opposite. In 1988, Kartal Demirağ, detained in connection with the assassination attempt against then President Turgut Özal, was found — according to Uğur Tönik, the prosecutor investigating the assassination attempt — to have served as deputy chairman of the Ülkü Ocakları in his hometown of Afyon Dazkırı, and to have trained as a young man at camps alongside MHP youth. The Free Thought Clubs

Another right-wing organization notable for its founders and members was the Free Thought Clubs (HDK). The first HDK was founded in 1964 at the Faculty of Political Science, in opposition to leftist students' SBF Idea Club. Under the chairmanship of Hasan Celal Güzel,76 the HDK began operating first in Ankara's other faculties and later in other provinces. During Güzel's chairmanship, the deputy chairman was Esat Güçhan,77 and the secretary-general was Murat Karayalçın.78 Other prominent names included İbrahim Melih Gökçek,79 Abdülkadir Aksu,80 Mehmet Keçeciler,81 Veysel Atasoy,82 Ahmet Mesut Yılmaz,83 and Hüsnü Doğan. In 1967, the HDK became a federation and took the name Federation of Free Thought Clubs. The federation's first chairman was Hasan Celal Güzel. Murat Karayalçın was elected chairman after Güzel.

Güzel says that the HDK, at an SBF dominated by the left, took a stance "along a line running from the center to the right" and that it was "genuinely a difficult undertaking."84 According to Güzel, this difficulty lay in the fact that the HDK — under his chairmanship, and holding "libertarian and liberal views" — "remained weak in this struggle" against an "internationalist socialist whirlwind."85 An attempt was made to remedy this weakness through nationalism:86

"The 'Free Thought Club,' with its libertarian and liberal outlook, which I chaired, was remaining weak in this struggle. I'll never forget: one day, Murat Karayalçın, who was our association's secretary-general at the time, and my deputy chairman Esat Güçhan, told me they wanted us to adopt a more nationalist atmosphere as well, and to put out a magazine and sell it in Kızılay the way the Marxists did."

Hasan Celal Güzel, Radikal, 11 February 2007

In response, they began publishing a magazine called "Milli Düşünce" [National Thought] and took part in founding an "Ülkü Ocağı" at the faculty. Güzel says they "handed over" the management of the Ülkü Ocağı they had founded to Yılmaz Yalçıner,87 as he puts it.88 Yılmaz Yalçıner, who took part in founding the Ülkü Ocağı, was one of the leading figures of idealist youth before gradually moving toward an Islamic line; Mehmet Şahin, a co-founder alongside Yalçıner, would years later become Rector of Erciyes University.89

The Islamist Movement After 27 May

The years in which the broad outlines of Turkey's still-ongoing political divisions first formed and emerged are, above all, the 1960s. It was largely in this period that left- and right-wing structures, in the sense we know them today, took shape, and that the terms "left" and "right" took on their present-day meaning within Turkey's political vocabulary. Where one could previously speak of an anti-communist stance, with the existing divide taking the form of "communist" versus "nationalist-traditionalist," this opposition would, from the second half of the 1960s onward, give way to a left-right conflict.90 It was also in this period that Islamists emerged onto the political stage as distinct from the nationalists, forming their own organizations. Zübeyir Yetik, one of the founders of the Association for the Struggle Against Communism, describes this quite openly in his memoir Geçmişten Notlar. Yetik states that in the early 1960s, despite the differences among them, everyone — nationalist, conservative, traditionalist, and religious alike — was grouped together and called "nationalist-traditionalist," and that they, too, presented themselves this way and moved as one.91 But in the second half of the 1960s, a split began, along with a struggle to seize control of the organizations.

Ali Gevgilili, in trying to explain the background of this split, refers to Şerif Mardin and notes that Islam in Turkey had differentiated into a dual structure — "religion as seen at the institutional level, Orthodox Sunni Islam along the lines of the ulema-i rusum [official religious scholars' thinking]," and "a second, Islamic [sic] cultural channel with the character of a folk religion" — before observing that Islam's assumption of an increasingly socio-political function grew after the 1950s. He identifies what set the 1950s apart as "the development of modern relations of production in society." He states that religion reorganized itself through associations, and also tried to create its own communication outlets in the press.92

Between 1946 and 1968, organizations such as Quran course, imam-hatip high school, and mosque-building associations accounted for more than a quarter of all associations. Alongside such religious associations, structures like the "Association for the Struggle Against Communism" (KMD) also spread rapidly from the 1960s onward. The number of KMD branches increased fifteenfold between 1963 and 1968, reaching 141.93

There are also views, distinct from the one Gevgilili draws attention to, that attempt to explain Islam's post-1950 assumption of a socio-political function through the wave of migration into Turkey.94 According to these views, migrants — under the additional influence of social change that was feared to fundamentally alter family relations — held fast to an Islamic identity, with religious communities and orders serving as harbors in which the masses took refuge in the cities. These studies note that migration's impact on political life made itself felt still more clearly after the 1960s, once the second generation entered the picture.

This organizing, carried out through associations and similar channels, would naturally come to be sought after by existing political structures, while at the same time striving to build its own political structure. The "nationalist-communalist split" also took place within this process. In recounting the events of 1968, Ali Gevgilili draws attention to two episodes. The first is the "Grand Congress of Religion and Traditionalists," held on 24–25 February 1968, at which "the Islamists [sic]… were by then debating their own strategy and tactics." The second episode Gevgilili highlights is the congress the Associations for the Struggle Against Communism held in İzmir on 31 June 1968. At this congress, "Türkeş's nationalist team… waged its final battle for control against the Islamists and the AP members." But they lost this battle and were pushed out of the association. Gevgilili also links the founding of the Ülkü Ocakları to this purge: "The Ülkü Ocakları, meant to form the 'striking force' [sic] of nationalist ideology among the youth, would be founded not long after this purge."95 Another indication of this split came at the Milli Türk Talebe Birliği's 1969 congress, when Burhanettin Kayhan — the candidate of youth with "heightened religious and spiritual sensitivities" — won election against Mustafa Ok, nicknamed "Komando" and backed by the MHP.96

The split that took place within the Associations for the Struggle Against Communism and the Milli Türk Talebe Birliği would also make itself felt in parliament, after Necmettin Erbakan ran as an independent candidate in the 1969 election, was elected as a deputy from Konya, and, on 26 January 1970, founded the Milli Nizam Partisi [National Order Party].97

The Milli Türk Talebe Birliği (MTTB) [National Turkish Students' Union]

The MTTB, whose founding dates back to 1916, was shut down in 1936, reopened in 1946 under the name Türk Talebe Birliği [Turkish Students' Union], and took back the name MTTB by decision of the Council of Ministers on 17 December 1947.

The MTTB's own website states that the Union was founded by the Committee of Union and Progress, that in the 1916–1920 period it acted in line with the Committee's Pan-Turkist outlook, and that in the 1926–1936 period, "in line with the official view of the republican cadres," it "rejected cosmopolitanism while remaining on a Turanist line" and launched the "Citizen, Speak Turkish!" campaign.98

From its refounding in 1946 until 1960, the MTTB followed a nationalist, anti-communist line. An MTTB declaration published on 10 December 1947 states that communism needed to be fought more fiercely: "Our association, on the basis of the nationalist outlook it has embraced as an article of faith, adopts as its motto a more fierce struggle against communism, which denies national sacred concepts such as homeland, nation, and family."99 The quotations below further illustrate this period of the MTTB.

"After this date, nationalist youth carried out their activities against Communism gathered around the MTTB. Until 1950, the MTTB organized numerous protests and programs against communism."

During DP rule, the MTTB's stance was "neither fully in step with, nor against, the government."102 From 1957 onward, it took a position against the government and closer to the CHP. The MTTB was shut down after the 12 September coup. Efforts to re-establish it began in 2006. It was founded by "a total of eight active university student associations across four provinces, Istanbul foremost among them, with the aim of reviving the MTTB," and a meeting was held on 16 December 2006 "on the occasion of the MTTB's 90th founding anniversary." Abdullah Gül, Bülent Arınç, Abdülkadir Aksu, Hüseyin Çelik, and Ali Coşkun also attended this meeting.118 As a result of these efforts, the MTTB was re-established on 27 March 2008 under the name "Talebe Birliği Federasyonu" [Students' Union Federation], later taking back the name MTTB.

In the end, in 1960s Turkey, and in the events of Kanlı Pazar, the MTTB, too, took its place on the stage as one of the parties to the conflict.

Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, Abdullah Gül, and the MTTB

When this book went to press, Turkey's President was Gül, and its Prime Minister was Erdoğan. Both men's paths crossed with the MTTB from the 1960s onward, and both were active within it. One encounters both names in the MTTB's activities in the '70s, in the Birlik Vakfı — founded with the same mission when the MTTB was shut down after 12 September — and in the MTTB's new period beginning in 2008. The political lives of these two MTTB members, which began in the 1960s, are important for showing the trajectory over the years of the MTTB — one of the central figures of Kanlı Pazar — and the place they came to occupy within the state. For this reason, the political lives of the two men will be examined alongside the MTTB's own story.

Recep Tayyip Erdoğan

Born in 1954, Erdoğan graduated from primary school in the 1964–1965 school year and enrolled at Istanbul İmam Hatip High School in 1965. Erdoğan's first political activities also began that year, when he took on a role in the MTTB's "secondary education" division.122 Turan Yılmaz, a reporter for the newspaper Hürriyet, relates in his book Tayyip — Kasımpaşa'dan Siyasetin Ön Saflarına [Tayyip: From Kasımpaşa to the Front Lines of Politics] that, because of this "political involvement" that began in his middle-school years, Erdoğan used to tell friends, "My political career is older than my teacher Erbakan's."123 Here, Erdoğan is alluding to Erbakan's entry into active politics in 1969 under the "Independents' Movement," implying that his own political involvement began before 1969.

"During his university years, Abdullah Gül actively carried on his intellectual struggle under the roof of the Milli Türk Talebe Birliği. Photographs of Gül and his friends were posted on walls by leftist groups, and they were prevented from entering the university for months."

These books recount how Deniz Gezmiş and his friends put up photographs of leading right-wing youth figures on the university's walls, labeled "fascist," that Abdullah Gül was one of those whose photograph was put up, and that Gül was unable to enter the university for six months because of these posters.131

Another, lesser-known side of Gül, which he mentioned in one interview, is that he was a founder of the Türk Ocağı and the Milliyetçiler Derneği [Nationalists' Association]. In that interview, saying "I too am a nationalist," Gül cited his founding of these two organizations and his tenure as chairman of the MTTB Executive Council as evidence of his nationalism.132

Between 1976 and 1978, he was sent to Exeter, England, for his doctoral studies on a scholarship from the Milli Kültür Vakfı. His biography states that he continued to be socially active there as well, founding the Türk Öğrenciler Yardımlaşma Derneği [Turkish Students' Mutual Aid Association] under the umbrella of the Federation of Student Islamic Societies (FOSIS). After completing his doctorate, he began working as a faculty member at Sakarya University.

Gül was one of the 650,000 people133 detained after the 12 September 1980 military coup. He was held in detention for a month. During this period, he was questioned about events from his student years. Gül states that he did not encounter "ill treatment" during this month-long interrogation. His brother Macit Gül, however, detained in Kayseri, was not as fortunate.134

Gül taught economics at Sakarya University until 1983, when he left the university and began working as an economics specialist at the Islamic Development Bank, headquartered in Jeddah. Gül's doctoral thesis was titled "The Development of Economic Relations Between Turkey and Islamic Countries." Debates Over the Claim That Erdoğan's Diploma Is Fake

Although the debate over Erdoğan's university diploma became especially prominent in the public eye during the 2014 presidential candidacy process, the issue dates back to the mid-2000s. Yalçın Küçük is said to have raised claims about Erdoğan's diploma in his books and talks from 2006 onward, and Banu Avar in 2007–2008. The debate took on a more political and legal dimension from 2014 onward, because the Constitution requires presidential candidates to be university graduates.

A few core issues lie at the heart of the debate. One is the legal status of the institution Erdoğan is said to have graduated from. It is known that Erdoğan enrolled in 1974 at the Aksaray School of Economics and Commerce, affiliated with the Istanbul Academy of Economic and Commercial Sciences. Yet the diploma samples shared with the public bear the name "Marmara University Faculty of Economics and Administrative Sciences," and the fact that Marmara University only took on its present structure after 1982 has been made a point of contention.

Another point of contention is that the original diploma has never been shared with the public. During the 2014 presidential election, it was announced that Erdoğan had submitted a notarized copy of his diploma to the Supreme Election Council (YSK). It later came to public attention, however, that the Turkish Union of Notaries had issued a disciplinary penalty against the notary in question, on the grounds that he had certified the copy without having seen the original diploma. This played a significant role in keeping the diploma debate alive.

The debate remained politically alive in subsequent years. Politicians from the CHP and various opposition circles called for Erdoğan's diploma records to be made public; various legal scholars and political parties filed applications with the Supreme Election Council. In 2016, Ömer Faruk Eminağaoğlu, former head of YARSAV, applied to the YSK claiming that "a forged official document" had been used in Erdoğan's candidacy process; the YSK rejected the application, stating that Erdoğan had submitted a notarized copy of his diploma.

The issue also resurfaced during the 2018 and 2023 presidential elections. In 2018, CHP candidate Muharrem İnce brought the issue back into the spotlight during his election campaign; the Halkın Kurtuluş Partisi [People's Liberation Party], in subsequent years, requested information from various institutions and, failing to obtain results, filed legal applications. Ahead of the 2023 election, a diploma sample said to belong to Erdoğan, along with a temporary graduation certificate and university entry document, was shared with the public by Presidential Communications Director Fahrettin Altun. According to the fact-checking organization Teyit's assessment, these documents show that Erdoğan entered university in 1974 and graduated in February 1981; but points such as the fact that the original diploma has never been shared with the public, the discrepancy between the institution's name in 1981 and its name on the diploma sample dated 1991, and the absence of a photograph on the temporary graduation certificate have kept the debate going.

Erdoğan, for his part, has rejected the diploma debate in statements made at various times, saying that "they have found and gotten his old schoolmates to speak," and that his records and graduation information exist. Marmara University, too, has stated in announcements made at various times that Erdoğan's graduation records are held in the university's archives. Despite this, the debate has continued for many years, for reasons including the institution's name on the diploma samples, the graduation date, the notarization process, and the fact that the original diploma has never been shared with the public.

The Associations for the Struggle Against Communism

The first attempt to found an Association for the Struggle Against Communism took place in Zonguldak in 1948. This effort was led by Necdet Sancar, Ziya Özkaynak, Bahattin Yurteri, Kani Engin, Zeki Kandemiroğlu, İsmail Tek, Tahsin Baysal, and Bahaddin Dökerel. Although the effort to found the association began in 1948, the association itself was not founded until 1950. Its first chairman was Dökerel. The association was revived and became active thanks to Necdet Sancar136 and Ziya Özkaynak, both of whom were high school teachers.137 At the second congress, held on 27 September 1951, Talip Taşman became chairman and Necdet Sancar deputy chairman. The association also engaged in publishing activity, putting out works titled "A Response to A. E. Yalman on the Nazım Hikmet Question," "An Open Letter to Turkish Youth," "What Is a Communist," "The Inner Truth of Communism," "The Tale of the Red Paradise," and "Mehmet Emin Yurdakul." It also published a biweekly newspaper called "Turkishness Against Communism." This association, founded in Zonguldak, ceased its activities in 1953 upon joining the Türk Milliyetçiler Derneği [Turkish Nationalists' Association].138

The second attempt to found the association took place in Istanbul. The association founded in Istanbul on 7 December 1956 had Burhanettin Şener as chairman and Altan Deliorman as secretary-general. In his piece "Errors in Pan-Turkism," Altan Deliorman periodizes the Türkiye Komünizmle Mücadele Derneği (TKMD) as the "First TKMD," "Second TKMD," and "Third TKMD," with the first being the 1948 attempt.139

The association founded in Istanbul explained its purpose as follows:

"To struggle against communism through ideas, by reinforcing the elements that form and strengthen our national constitution and enable us to live as a nation. To achieve this aim, to root deeply our attachment to history, homeland, and God."

"Friends, fellow citizens!

While Russia works to disturb world peace through a thousand and one political intrigues and provocations, it also strives, with the aim of wearing down the constitution of free nations, to spread the poisonous seeds of its repugnant regime.

The association ceased its activities after 27 May and was refounded in 1963, headquartered in İzmir. Altan Deliorman calls this period of the association the Third TKMD. During this period of the association, already at its founding stage, tension arose between the "nationalist" faction and the "traditionalist" faction. Zübeyir Yetik — who had spearheaded the founding of the association, being "the one who conceived, started, and carried through this undertaking" — was kept out of the association as the leading figure of the traditionalist faction, and the nationalist faction carried out the founding. The traditionalist faction, Yetik included, nonetheless continued its activities within the association. Yetik attributes the attempt to exclude them from the association's work to the association's relations with America. According to Yetik's account, it was discussed and agreed within the association that they would apply to the American Consulate in order to find financial resources and support. Yetik describes what followed like this:142

"As it turned out, our people had met with the Consulate and conveyed their requests. Because, as holders of an Islam-based outlook, we apparently had to be neutralized at all costs."

Working from this information, and taking into account that he was left off the list of founders, that he was prevented from setting up a branch, and that he was not even accepted as a member, Yetik says he concluded that "this hand had been involved in the matter from the very start."

The activities of this new İzmir-based association spread rapidly after İlhan Egemen Darendelioğlu, owner of the magazine Toprak, took over its chairmanship in 1965;143 the number of branches, 27 in 1965, rose to 110 within a short time. From 1965 onward, the association organized rallies in many provinces. President Cemal Gürsel even served, if only for a few days, as the association's honorary chairman, until his resignation on 16 July 1965.144 During this period, radio spots aired declaring, "by order of the Presidency," "Citizens, beware! Water sleeps, but communism does not," "The communist infiltrates your ranks disguised as a Turk and a Muslim," and "Communism is a snake that stabs you in the back with sweet words."145

The Mücadele Birliği (MB) [Struggle Union]

Called, on the one hand, "the right's Dev-Genç"152 and alleged, on the other, to have been set up by the state,153 the Mücadele Birliği — though its formation dates back to around 1964154 — was officially founded on 18 November 1967. Its founders were three imams and one student from a Higher Islamic Institute. One of the three founding imams, a former "student leader," was Necmettin Erişen, who took on the role of chairman. The "ideologue," the "intellectual father" of this structure, was Aykut Edibali.155 İrfan Küçükköy, who took part in the association's founding efforts, lists its principles as "anti-Zionist, anti-communist, anti-capitalist, loyal to national values, respectful of Islam."156

The MB was organized under the names "Sancak" [Banner] and "Nationalist Culture Unions." Sancaks were established in provinces with a developed membership base. The MB had three Sancaks: Konya, Afyon, and Istanbul. In provinces without a Sancak, Nationalist Culture Unions were established instead.

The MB is described as a structure that did not shy away from violence but stayed clear of armed conflict. Aykut Edibali, the MB's leading figure, expressed this by saying, "I never so much as bloodied anyone's nose; I watched over them like my own children, I never let blood be spilled, whether by selling it or having it sold."157 Within the MB, however, a "striking force" called the "Peace and Order Committee" was formed in February 1969, and a camp was set up for its training in the village of Gürpınar, near Büyükçekmece, Istanbul.158 Erişen ties the meaning of the fist in the MB's symbol to the slogan they used at the time: "He is no Muslim who will not use his fist for his faith."159

A piece appearing in the 23 February 1971 issue of Yeniden Milli Mücadele [Renewed National Struggle], the Union's publication, defending the provocations and attacks in Konya and Kayseri as well as Kanlı Pazar, is enough on its own to give a sense of the Union:

"The nation, having shown its strength and its inclination in Konya, Kayseri, and Taksim, waits with patient excitement, certain of its victory, to throw everything un-national into the trash. There is no power that can delay the nation's victory."

160

There was also, for a time, a clash between MB supporters and MTTB students. Standing in the same camp did not prevent conflict between them. Over a dispute stemming from the Conquest celebrations, MB supporters attacked MTTB members on 29 May 1970. In response, Burhanettin Kayhan, then MTTB chairman, issued a statement declaring that the MTTB had no connection to the MB and that the MB was on the wrong path.161

The debates over whether the MB was set up by the state came to the fore particularly in the 1970s. Cemil Çiçek, who had been part of the MB's ranks, states in an interview in 2002 that not just the MB but all "groups of that kind" were founded "with the state's knowledge and involvement," in these words:162

"Indeed, it's become clear that, under the conditions of '68, during that Cold War period, these were organizations operating with the state's close involvement and knowledge. I was there for a certain period, and, having seen certain things as a result, I was among the earliest to leave. (…) Groups of that sort were groups that had real strategic value under the conditions of the day. You'd come up from Yozgat, from Samsun, from Trabzon, to Istanbul. Everyone was saying, 'Russia is going to occupy Turkey, the Turkish state is in danger. O people of the homeland, rise up, take your place, and see off this danger.' And you, too, would join organizations of this kind in good faith. Now here's the strange thing about this state: it has these kinds of organizations set up, and then turns around and asks, 'Why did you get involved in this?'"

Cemil Çiçek, Yeni Şafak, 16 June 2002

İrfan Küçükköy, who also wrote a book titled Mücadele Birliği, states that some of his friends within the movement had close ties to intelligence figures, and describes relationships with Hiram Abbas, Mehmet Eymür, and Abdullah Çatlı. Even so, to the question "Was it set up by the state?" he answers: "They were sympathetic to us because we were defending the state, the nation, the army — but it was absolutely not a movement they set up."163 Saadettin Tantan, a police officer assigned at the time to monitor the MB, is alleged to have used the words, when the MB came up, "Oh, you mean that organization Ziya Uygur set up?"164 The Ziya Uygur Tantan refers to was a state official who had been expelled from the army over the "3 May 1944 Turkism events"165 but who continued to serve in military intelligence.

The academic Mümtaz'er Türköne, who was himself part of right-wing youth in the 1970s, speaks quite categorically about the MB. According to Türköne, the MB was set up, from beginning to end, according to standards laid down by the Special Warfare Department.166

In the 1970s, the MB began to fragment, and splits occurred, as the nationalist line grew increasingly dominant within it. Since 23 November 1992, the Millet Partisi [Nation Party], led by Aykut Edibali, has continued to exist as the MB's successor.

"Şulebaşı," the Forerunner of the Türban, and the First University Boycott

1968 was the year boycotts and occupations began at universities and steadily escalated. Although it was leftist youth who carried out these boycotts, the first boycott was launched for a different reason, by a different group: at the Faculty of Theology in Ankara, the first boycott arose over the expulsion of Hatice Babacan, who wore a headscarf, and Mustafa Demirsöz, who had argued with and "insulted" his instructor, Assoc. Prof. Dr. Bahriye Üçok, in class. The MTTB was active in the boycott. Speaking at a press conference held on the matter, İsmail Kahraman protested Babacan's expulsion, saying, "This is a decision that will go down as a disgrace in university history."168 Despite the MTTB's prominent role, leftist students also lent their support to the action.169

These events, and the developments that followed, are described as follows in the 22nd issue, dated May 1968, of the magazine "Milli Hareket," then being published:170

The situation at the Faculty of Theology

Events developed rapidly at this faculty, where a boycott began after two students were expelled. After Mustafa Demirsöz, who had received an expulsion penalty, pitched a tent in front of the faculty and began a hunger strike, the faculty's students published a declaration setting out the situation and their views; not long after, the dean resigned, in what amounted to an admission of guilt. Some time later, the faculty was put on a month-long recess to let things settle down, but the students, arguing that this recess was deliberate, began coming to the faculty anyway, with upperclassmen teaching classes to first- and second-year students. The situation began taking on a genuinely remarkable character for Turkey. The declaration published by the faculty's students reads as follows:

(…) Again, this same female associate professor [Bahriye Üçok], after provoking Mustafa Demirsöz, a second-year student who said that the ideas she instilled during class reflected an orientalist view and that no such thing existed in Islamic sources, by insulting and belittling him with words such as backward, anti-Western, primitive, and bigoted, then complained to the Administrative Board upon receiving his response and secured his EXPULSION.171

(…) Although the real reason for Hatice Babacan's expulsion was her refusal to remove her headscarf, the decision tied her expulsion to an ostensible reason: "making statements or engaging in conduct, whether inside or outside the school or through the press, that damages the school's moral standing."

Milli Hareket magazine, May 1968, No. 22

After her expulsion from the Faculty of Theology, Babacan enrolled at Ankara's Faculty of Language, History and Geography, and graduated from that faculty wearing her headscarf throughout.172

This episode draws particular attention both as one of the first indications of the türban [headscarf] issue that would go on to occupy an important place on Turkey's agenda in later years, and for the resonance the names involved carry today.

At the time, the term "türban" did not yet exist. In the students' declaration, Hatice Babacan is described as "covering her head with a scarf." But it was in those days — or rather, in those years — that the foundation of the "türban" issue was laid. Before Hatice Babacan, Dr. Hümeyra Ökten in the 1950s, and Gülsen Ataseven — a medical student who finished top of her class in 1964 but was barred from giving the traditional valedictory speech — had covered their heads, but these incidents were seen as "isolated" and drew no wider response. From the mid-1960s onward, however, the issue of covering one's head entered the public agenda together with the name Şule Yüksel Şenler.

Setting the "matchmaking" aspect aside, these details are interesting for what they reveal about the positions and relationships of figures who would go on to occupy important places in Turkey's official politics in the 2000s.

As one final resonance, we might mention the name Bahriye Üçok. Accounts of Bahriye Üçok's life generally use the stock phrase, "She began receiving threats from the 1960s onward, and had to take a break from her academic work because she no longer felt safe."179 Having taken a break from her academic life, Üçok was elected an appointed senator by President Cevdet Sunay in 1971, served on the Presiding Board of the Senate of the Republic for five years, joined the CHP in 1977, became a founding member of the Halkçı Parti [Populist Party] established after 12 September, was elected as a deputy from Ordu in the 1984 election, and became a member of the SHP in 1986. She lost her life on 6 October 1990 when a bomb exploded as she tried to open a parcel bomb mailed to her home. At the time of her death, it was known that Üçok, as a faculty member who had served at the Faculty of Theology, had been receiving threats because she had stated that "covering and fasting are not obligatory in Islam."180

What Hatice Babacan and Şule Yüksel Şenler experienced in those years offers at least limited insight into the state of "Islamic" activity at the time. Islamists were increasingly making themselves visible in political life, emerging with their own demands, and — perhaps more significantly — coming to public attention through demands touching on everyday life, in other words, becoming popular.
